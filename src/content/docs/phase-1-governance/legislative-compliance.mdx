State AI law is moving faster than federal AI law and is what many agencies will be asked about in the next 24 months. This page catalogs the U.S. federal and state laws most likely to affect a state or local government AI deployment as of `Last Updated: 2026-04-30`, what each generally requires, and which Phase 1 artifact gives you a starting point.

> **Verify before relying.** This page is a map, not legal advice or legal authority. Statutes are amended, rules are issued, court interpretations shift, and federal requirements may apply to state/local agencies only when incorporated into a grant, contract, state policy, or local rule. Confirm current status with counsel before relying on any row.

## Local

Many cities have passed surveillance technology ordinances that apply to AI even when state law has not. The most cited:

- **Seattle, San Francisco, Oakland, Berkeley, Cambridge MA:** surveillance-technology ordinances (CCOPS-style) requiring council approval and a public-impact report before adopting AI-bearing surveillance technologies.
- **New York City:** Local Law 35 (algorithmic tools transparency) and the AEDT requirements above.
- **Boston, San Jose, Tempe:** published city AI guidelines that apply to staff use even where state law is silent.
- **City of Austin** runs a TRUST Act-style surveillance review for facial recognition and similar technologies. Several other jurisdictions (Oakland CCOPS, Seattle Surveillance Ordinance) have parallel processes.

If your jurisdiction has a surveillance ordinance or CCOPS-style review, AI use cases that involve face matching, license-plate recognition, gunshot detection, or similar capabilities trigger that ordinance _in addition to_ this Phase 1 governance.

## How to use this page

1. **Identify your jurisdiction.** Long-standing federal statutes may apply broadly; OMB memoranda bind covered federal agencies directly; state and local rows apply only where your agency is in scope.
2. **Cross-reference each applicable law to its Phase 1 artifact.** Where a Phase 1 artifact appears to cover a requirement, record that mapping and ask counsel whether the artifact needs local language.
3. **Identify gaps.** Where an applicable law has a requirement no Phase 1 artifact addresses (e.g., a state-specific public-notice template), document the gap and assign an owner before deployment of any AI use case affected.
4. **Update your AUP.** Section 4(5) of the AUP cites "applicable state AI law"; replace `{{Applicable State AI Law}}` with the controlling statute(s) for your jurisdiction.
5. **Schedule a right-sized review.** Small agencies can re-check before AI purchases or quarterly if active. Medium and large agencies should put the matrix on a quarterly committee agenda.
