## Objective

Establish the guardrails that enable everything else. Without governance, infrastructure requests stall, vendors can't be evaluated, and any public-facing AI deployment carries unmanaged political risk.

> **Use this as an implementation guide, not legal authority.** Phase 1 describes the baseline governance controls agencies should put in place before AI work scales: risk tiers, an AUP, a review path, procurement guardrails, and an inventory. The artifacts can be simplified, sequenced, or localized to fit staffing and legal requirements; counsel decides what is binding in your jurisdiction.

## How this phase scales for your agency

<Tabs syncKey="agency-size">
<TabItem label="Small">

**2-day governance sprint with counsel or a shared legal resource.** Use a city/county attorney, regional association, or contract attorney with public-sector AI experience for a focused intensive that produces the AUP, the 3-tier risk classification, and a lightweight review charter. Adopt the templates with minimal edits rather than redrafting from scratch.

- **Deliverables:** AUP (adopted), risk classification (adopted), committee charter (signed), procurement addendum (attached to standard contract template).
- **Simplify:** One named AI owner, ad-hoc review meetings, a starter spreadsheet inventory, and the published compliance matrix as a reference instead of a locally maintained legal tracker.
- **Budget:** $3K–$8K (single sprint engagement).
- **Off-ramp:** This is a complete starting governance baseline. It covers common policy needs and gives counsel concrete artifacts to review when a law, grant, or procurement requires more.

</TabItem>
<TabItem label="Standard">

**Five working sessions across Months 1–3.** Convene a small Review Committee or working group (technology owner, legal/procurement, security or records, HR, program sponsor) and run them through the full deliverable list using the templates in this phase.

- **Deliverables:** Everything listed below: 3-tier risk classification, AUP, committee charter, procurement guardrails, legislative compliance matrix, use case inventory.
- **Cadence:** Committee meets every other week through launch if the workload justifies it; otherwise monthly with ad-hoc reviews for urgent Tier-2 or Tier-3 items.
- **Budget:** $5K–$25K (staff time + external legal review).

</TabItem>
<TabItem label="Large">

**Full sessions plus standing committee with sub-groups.** Run the same five working sessions, but split the committee into two working groups (policy + procurement) that meet in parallel. Add a quarterly compliance review and an annual policy refresh.

- **Deliverables:** Everything in the Standard path, plus jurisdiction-specific procurement guardrails, multi-jurisdiction compliance matrix (if you operate across states), and an annual policy refresh process.
- **Cadence:** Standing committee meets monthly; sub-groups meet weekly during launch.
- **Budget:** Skews toward the high end of $25K and into the dedicated policy-counsel range when multiple offices, jurisdictions, or high-impact systems are in scope.

</TabItem>

## Deliverables

- 3-tier AI risk classification (Low / Medium / High) with examples mapped to agency operations
- AI Acceptable Use Policy (based on Mississippi/ISACA templates, adapted for agency context)
- AI Review Committee charter (3–8 members, depending on agency size)
- AI procurement guardrails (M-25-22-informed for federal alignment, adapted for state/local use)
- Legislative compliance matrix (state, federal, and local references for counsel review)
- AI use case inventory template (starter fields plus expanded compliance fields for agencies that need them)

## Off-Ramp — Governance Only

Agencies that complete only this phase walk away with an adopted AUP, a 3-tier risk classification, a chartered review path, a starter inventory, and procurement guardrails attached to the standard contract template. That set is a complete, standalone governance baseline and covers many common policy requirements. Infrastructure build-out, education tracks beyond Track 1, and platform work are deferred; document them so they re-enter the roadmap when budget and staffing allow.

## Hard dependencies

- **Phase 1 is the upstream blocker for Phase 3 (D-01).** Cloud provisioning needs governance approval, the AUP defines what data can flow where, and the procurement addendum gates vendor terms; without these, sandbox requests stall.
- **Phase 1 gates any Tier-2 or Tier-3 production deployment (D-08).** Higher-tier workloads cannot ship without the risk classification, AUP, and committee in place.
- **Risk classification must exist before Phase 3's security baseline.** The baseline maps tier to enforced policy (network posture, logging retention, contestation pathway); without tiers there is nothing to enforce.

## What Phase 1 does NOT cover

- Specific application code or architecture (that's Phase 4–5).
- Specific model selection (Claude, GPT, Gemini, open weights). That decision happens in Phase 5 against the platform's AI orchestration adapter.
- The actual training of staff on the policy. Phase 2 / Track 3 owns delivery once the artifacts exist.
- Case-by-case review of pending vendor contracts. The Review Committee owns that operationally; Phase 1 stands the committee up, it does not run its docket.

## Suggested order

1. **Adopt the Risk Classification Matrix first.** Every other Phase 1 artifact references the tiers.
2. **Stand up the Review Committee.** Charter, members, first meeting on the calendar. The committee will own everything else.
3. **Draft and adopt the AUP.** The committee enforces it; the matrix gives it tier-by-tier teeth.
4. **Attach the Procurement Addendum** to your standard contract template before the next AI procurement.
5. **Localize the Legislative Compliance Matrix** for your jurisdiction and update your AUP's `{{Applicable State AI Law}}` placeholder.

You can run steps 1–3 in parallel if you have the people. Step 4 depends on legal sign-off; step 5 is a quarterly maintenance task once started.

## Large-agency extension: legal-boundary layer

The 3-tier risk model above is what every agency uses to classify proposed AI uses. Most state and local agencies stop there.

Some large agencies, especially those with strong general counsel offices and active legislative oversight, find they need a second layer of classification on top of the risk tiers. The reason: some AI uses are not just "high risk." They are uses that the agency may not legally undertake without a specific legislative grant of authority. Treating those uses as "Tier 3 high-risk" understates the legal posture and confuses the review process.

The legal-boundary layer is orthogonal to the risk tiers. Every proposed use gets two labels:

- **Risk tier**: 1 (low), 2 (moderate), or 3 (high). Drives the depth of review and the documentation required.
- **Legal status**: permitted, requires authorization, or prohibited absent legislative authorization. Drives whether the review committee can approve the use at all, or whether it must be referred to general counsel and legislative liaison.

A use that is Tier 1 (low risk) AND prohibited absent legislative authorization still cannot be deployed. A use that is Tier 3 (high risk) AND permitted can be deployed with the full Tier 3 review burden.

Examples of the "prohibited absent legislative authorization" category:
- Algorithmic eligibility determinations for benefits where statute requires an individualized human determination.
- Automated decisions affecting custody, parole, or other liberty interests where state code reserves the decision to a named officer.
- Any use that would create a new public record category that statute has not authorized the agency to maintain.

Small and standard agencies usually do not need this layer. The 3-tier risk model is enough because their general counsel handles legal-boundary questions case by case during Tier 3 reviews. Large agencies adopt the legal-boundary layer when the volume of proposed uses makes case-by-case counsel review a bottleneck.

This framing originated with state agencies operating under explicit statutory authority constraints. Other large agencies (federal, larger cities) may adopt similar layered models. See the [state-agency case study](/resources/case-study-large-state/) for one worked example.

## Plain-English Guide to Phase 1 Terms

- **AUP (Acceptable Use Policy).** The agency's plain-language rules for how staff, contractors, and vendors may use AI tools: what data can go in, what outputs are acceptable, who is accountable. The AUP is the document staff actually read; everything else in Phase 1 supports it.
- **Risk tier (Low / Medium / High).** A 3-level classification of an AI use case based on the harm it could cause if it goes wrong. Low is internal productivity (drafting, summarizing); Medium touches public-facing or operational decisions; High influences benefits, enforcement, or safety. The tier dictates the controls that apply.
- **Procurement guardrails.** Standard contract language and a vendor questionnaire that the agency attaches to any AI-related solicitation. They cover data rights, model provenance, audit access, and contestation: the items that are nearly impossible to retrofit after a contract is signed.
- **Committee charter.** The signed document that names the Review Committee's members, scope of authority, decision rules, and meeting cadence. Without a charter the committee is a meeting; with one it is a body that can approve, defer, or block AI work.
- **Legislative compliance.** The set of state and federal AI laws that apply to the agency: Texas TRAIGA, Colorado AI Act, California SB 942, OMB memoranda, and any local ordinance. The compliance matrix maps each requirement to the Phase 1 artifact that satisfies it.
- **OMB M-25-21 / M-25-22.** Two federal memoranda that bind covered federal agencies and serve as useful reference models for state and local agencies. They may apply indirectly when incorporated into a grant, contract, state policy, or local procurement requirement. M-25-21 (April 2025; supersedes M-24-10) covers AI use case inventory, impact assessment, and minimum risk practices. M-25-22 (April 2025; supersedes M-24-18) covers federal AI procurement and contract-planning expectations.
- **NIST AI RMF.** The National Institute of Standards and Technology's AI Risk Management Framework. A non-prescriptive playbook organized around four functions (Govern, Map, Measure, Manage) that the agency's AUP, risk classification, and committee operationalize.
