Session 3.2 is the working meeting where the agency's draft AUP gets written. Most of the model text is provided in the Phase 1 [Acceptable Use Policy](/phase-1-governance/acceptable-use-policy/) page; the session is about the agency-specific decisions that make the model text adoptable by your specific organization.

## Deliverable produced

A draft AUP ready for legal review. Includes the agency's name, scope, prohibited uses, exceptions, reporting paths, and accountability — all customized from the model text.

## Audience and prerequisites

Same Track 3 audience. Session 3.1 must be complete (the AUP references the tier matrix produced there). General Counsel attendance strongly recommended.

## Pre-work

- Read the [AUP page](/phase-1-governance/acceptable-use-policy/) in full.
- Each attendee skims the agency's existing Acceptable Use Policy (general technology) and notes 2–3 places where AI-specific language must extend it.
- The AI program lead pre-runs the [AUP wizard](/phase-1-governance/acceptable-use-policy/) and brings the exported draft as the working starting point.

## Materials

- AI deck source markdown: [Download the context deck source](/deck-sources/phase-2/track-3-governance/session-3-2-aup-drafting-context.md). Paste or upload it into your preferred AI presentation tool, then localize, verify, and brand the generated mini-deck before use.
- The Phase 1 AUP wizard, projected for live editing.
- The exported wizard draft, distributed to attendees.
- The agency's existing technology AUP, for cross-reference.
- The ratified risk matrix from Session 3.1.

## 15-minute context-setting

The facilitator walks through the AUP structure:

1. Scope and definitions
2. Approved AI Tools List
3. Prohibited uses
4. Tiered review pathway (references Session 3.1's matrix)
5. Data handling rules
6. Disclosure requirements
7. Incident reporting
8. Training and adoption requirements
9. Enforcement and exceptions

Names the three to five decisions the room needs to make today.

## 60-minute working session

### Block A — Scope and definitions (15 min)

- Confirm the agency name, the scope (which staff, which contractors, which volunteers), and the effective date.
- Confirm the integration with the existing technology AUP. Most agencies append AI-specific language as a section in the main AUP rather than a standalone document — but a standalone document is cleaner for a first version. The room decides.
- Resolve any agency-specific definitions that conflict with the model text.

### Block B — Prohibited uses and exceptions (20 min)

The model text lists prohibited uses (e.g., automated benefits denial without human review, AI-generated content presented as human-authored without disclosure, use of unapproved tools with sensitive data). The room reviews and:

- Confirms or extends the prohibited list with agency-specific items.
- Defines the **exception process** — who can grant exceptions, on what timeline, and what the documentation requirements are. A prohibited list without an exception path produces shadow AI; an exception path without documentation produces an unauditable mess.
- Decides on enforcement language. Most agencies tie violations to existing employee conduct policies rather than creating new disciplinary categories.

### Block C — Disclosure and reporting (15 min)

Two specific decisions:

1. **What disclosure is required by tier?** The model text says Tier-1 is none, Tier-2 is internal, Tier-3 is public. Confirm, or modify based on agency context (e.g., a hospital may require internal disclosure even for Tier-1 if patient-facing staff are involved).
2. **Where do staff report concerns?** Name the specific channel — HR, equity officer, ombuds, anonymous hotline — by office and contact. Generic "email the AI program lead" is rarely the right answer for sensitive reports.

### Block D — Adoption mechanics (10 min)

How does the AUP get adopted? The model text specifies:

- AI Review Committee approval.
- Approving Body ratification (city council, board, or executive sponsor depending on agency type).
- Annual review with documented amendments.
- New-hire acknowledgement during onboarding.
- All-staff acknowledgement at first publication.

The room confirms or adjusts the adoption pathway and names the target dates for each step.

## 15-minute ratification

Facilitator reads back the agency-specific edits. The wizard is updated live and exported. The output is:

- A clean v0.1 draft of the AUP with agency name and customizations applied.
- A list of named open items for legal review (typically 2–4).
- A target date for legal review completion and committee ratification (typically 3–4 weeks out).

## Common questions and how to handle them

- **"What about contractors and vendors?"** Contractors are bound by the AUP through their contract. Vendors are bound through the [Procurement Addendum](/phase-1-governance/procurement-guardrails/). Cross-reference.
- **"Do we need to negotiate with the union?"** If the agency is unionized, yes — see the [Job-Impact Messaging Kit](/phase-2-education/job-impact-messaging/). The AUP itself is typically a management policy, but adoption mechanics often involve labor.
- **"How do we update the Approved AI Tools List?"** The Review Committee maintains it. The AUP says how the list is published; the list itself is a separate maintained document.
- **"What about staff using personal AI tools at work?"** The AUP must address BYOD/personal-tool use specifically. Most agencies prohibit personal AI tools for any work data; some allow it for non-sensitive personal productivity. Decide and write it down.
