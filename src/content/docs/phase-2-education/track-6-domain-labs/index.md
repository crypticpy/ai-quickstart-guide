Track 6 is where AI literacy becomes _domain_ literacy. Generic AI training tells a case worker what a hallucination is; the Domain Lab teaches the same case worker how to spot a hallucinated SNAP eligibility cut-off using their own professional judgment. The lab format puts domain experts in the seat where the work actually happens — reviewing AI outputs against their own caseload, their own statutes, their own templates — and trains the evaluation reflex that no horizontal training can produce.

## Track at a glance

| Field        | Value                                                                                                                   |

| Audience     | Domain experts: case workers, inspectors, planners, analysts, attorneys, clinicians, etc.                               |
| Mandatory?   | Recommended for any domain expert whose role is on the [augmentation roadmap](/phase-2-education/job-impact-messaging/) |
| Format       | 2 × 3-hour applied labs per role family + ongoing monthly clinic                                                        |
| Cohort size  | 8–15 per role family (smaller is better for review-quality work)                                                        |
| Prerequisite | Track 1 complete; ≥1 year experience in the domain                                                                      |
| Timing       | Months 4–9, sequenced after Track 1 and after at least one approved tool exists for the role                            |
| ADKAR focus  | Knowledge and Ability — domain-specific evaluation skill                                                                |
| Kirkpatrick  | Levels 2–3 — measured skill in evaluation, applied in real work                                                         |

## Lab structure

Each role family gets two 3-hour applied labs and a recurring monthly clinic.

### Lab 1 — "Is This Output Correct?"

Domain experts review 8–12 real AI outputs from agency-relevant tasks (sanitized, with governance approval). For each output, they answer:

- Is this factually correct?
- Is this complete? What is missing?
- Is this defensible? Could it stand up to a contestation, a FOIA request, or a court review?
- What would I have written?
- What would I want the model to do differently next time?

The instructor (a senior domain expert + an AI program lead) facilitates discussion. The cohort builds, in the room, a **role-specific evaluation rubric** — the criteria they use to judge AI outputs going forward. This rubric becomes input to the [eval harness](/phase-2-education/track-4-developers/) for that role's tools.

### Lab 2 — "Where Does This Fit in My Workflow?"

The same cohort returns 2–3 weeks later, having tried at least one approved tool on real low-stakes work. They report:

- What did the tool actually do well?
- What did it do badly?
- Where does it fit in the workflow — front-end drafting, back-end review, side-channel research?
- What is the human-in-the-loop checkpoint?
- What would change in their job description if this tool became standard?

The output is a **role-specific integration map** — a written description of how AI fits into the domain expert's work — that feeds the [role evolution maps](/phase-2-education/job-impact-messaging/) and Phase 6 starter project scoping.

### Monthly clinic

After both labs, the role family meets monthly for 90 minutes. Format:

- 30 min — review of AI outputs from the past month (anonymized, real). What got better? What got worse?
- 30 min — case study: one cohort member walks through a specific decision they made with or without AI support, and the cohort critiques.
- 30 min — open questions and intake submissions.

The clinic is the _ongoing_ form of the labs. Domain expertise about AI is not learned once; it is calibrated continuously as models change and use cases mature.

## Role families covered

The exact list depends on the agency. The default starting set:

| Role family                                         | Lab focus examples                                                                    |
| --------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Case workers (eligibility, intake, social services) | Drafting determinations, summarizing case histories, identifying missed evidence      |
| Inspectors (code, health, environmental)            | Drafting findings, summarizing prior site visits, flagging anomalies                  |
| Planners and policy analysts                        | Drafting policy briefs, summarizing public comment, comparative jurisdiction research |
| Customer service and contact center                 | Suggested responses, knowledge base routing, after-call summaries                     |
| Attorneys and legal staff                           | First-draft research memos, document review, citation checking                        |
| IT and technical staff                              | Code drafts, log triage, documentation drafts                                         |
| Communications staff                                | Press release drafts, social media drafts, plain-language rewrites                    |

Agencies add role families as needed. Not every agency has every family — sequence Track 6 by the families that have approved tools available.

## Format

Each 3-hour lab runs:

- **30 min framing.** What we are evaluating today, why it matters, what the rubric will look like.
- **2 hours applied review.** Pairs work through the AI outputs. Instructor circulates. Discussion as patterns emerge.
- **30 min synthesis.** The cohort agrees on a written rubric or integration map.

Co-facilitation is essential: a senior domain expert (who carries credibility with the cohort) and an AI program staffer (who can answer technical questions about why the model produced what it did). Neither alone is sufficient.

## Output discipline

Track 6 produces evaluation artifacts:

- A role-specific evaluation rubric per role family, contributed back to Track 4's eval harness.
- A role-specific integration map per role family, contributed back to the job-impact role evolution maps.
- A monthly clinic record (lightweight) per role family.
- Intake submissions surfaced through the labs and clinics.

These artifacts are the difference between an agency that "uses AI" and an agency where domain experts can defend AI-assisted decisions to oversight, contestation, and the public.

## Pedagogical posture

Track 6 is the most domain-deferential track in the curriculum. The AI program lead's job is to facilitate, not to teach the domain. The senior domain co-facilitator drives the rubric work; the AI program lead supplies the model context and the eval framing. Cohorts who feel the AI program is teaching them their own job revolt — appropriately.

This is also why Track 6 cannot start until at least one approved tool exists for the role family. Theoretical evaluation does not produce a usable rubric. The cohort needs real outputs to react to.

## Evaluation

| Level | Metric                                                                                        | Target            |
| ----- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------- |
| 2     | Each cohort produces a written evaluation rubric and integration map                          | 100%              |
| 3     | Domain experts who attended Track 6 use the rubric in their work (self-reported + spot-check) | ≥80% at 60 days   |
| 3     | Tier-2 and Tier-3 review committee decisions cite Track 6 rubrics                             | ≥60% of decisions |
| 4     | AI-assisted work in Track-6-trained role families has measurably lower error rate vs. control | Yes               |

## Common Track 6 failures

- **Running Lab 1 without governance-approved outputs.** Real outputs require sanitization and Review Committee blessing. Don't shortcut this — using uncleared outputs in a training is itself a Tier-2-ish AI use case.
- **Letting the AI program lead drive the rubric.** The rubric must come from the domain experts. The program lead documents and frames; the domain experts decide.
- **Skipping the clinic.** The monthly clinic is where calibration happens. Without it, the rubrics rot as models change.
- **Treating Track 6 as advanced Track 1.** The audiences are different. Track 1 is awareness and posture; Track 6 is applied evaluation. Reusing Track 1 content for Track 6 wastes the cohort's time.
