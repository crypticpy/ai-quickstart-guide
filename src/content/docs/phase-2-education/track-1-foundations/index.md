Track 1 is the only track that runs for **every staff member**, technical or not. It moves the organization from "I've heard of AI" to "I see how this helps my work" — the ADKAR Awareness-to-Desire transition.

## Track at a glance

| Field        | Value                                                             |
| ------------ | ----------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Audience     | All agency staff                                                  |
| Mandatory?   | Yes (for anyone who will use AI tools or AI-influenced decisions) |
| Format       | 4 × 90-minute sessions                                            |
| Group size   | 15–25 (8–12 for small agencies)                                   |
| Prerequisite | None                                                              |
| Timing       | Months 1–2                                                        |
| ADKAR focus  | Awareness (Sessions 1.1–1.2), Desire (Sessions 1.3–1.4)           |
| Kirkpatrick  | Levels 1–3                                                        |

## Session structure (the same for all four)

Each session is 90 minutes, structured 30 / 30 / 30:

- **30 min presentation** — facilitator-led, slide-supported, but conversational. The slides are scaffolding, not script. The facilitator should be looking at the room, not the deck.
- **30 min demo or case study** — live AI interaction or structured walk-through. Real public data, never staff PII.
- **30 min hands-on activity** — attendees do something themselves. This is the part that produces the ADKAR shift.

Closing 5 minutes — circle the room. Each attendee names one thing they'll try in the next two weeks. This is not optional; it produces the behavioral commitment that drives Level 3 outcomes.

## Evaluation criteria

| Level        | Metric                                              | Target        |
| ------------ | --------------------------------------------------- | ------------- |
| 1 (Reaction) | Post-session survey average                         | 4.2 / 5.0     |
| 2 (Learning) | Quiz on terminology + risk categories (Session 1.4) | 80% pass rate |
| 3 (Behavior) | Attendees who submit ≥1 intake form within 30 days  | ≥50%          |
| 4 (Results)  | Viable use-case ideas generated per 100 attendees   | ≥10           |

## Facilitator profile

Track 1 facilitators do **not** need to be AI experts. They need to:

- Be comfortable in front of a non-technical audience.
- Know enough about AI to handle "what about deepfakes / privacy / bias" questions credibly. (One full read of the [Glossary](/getting-started/glossary/) is sufficient.)
- Be willing to say "I don't know — let me find out and follow up" rather than improvise an answer.

Most agencies use a mix: the AI program lead facilitates the first cohort, then trains 2–3 internal staff to facilitate subsequent cohorts. In small agencies, the IT lead facilitates from the provided scripts.

## Materials list

For each session, the facilitator needs:

- The session guide (linked in the table above) printed or open in a tab.
- Slide deck (provided as a markdown outline; rendered in PowerPoint, Keynote, Gamma, or Google Slides per agency preference).
- Three example AI tools accessible to attendees during the hands-on portion. For Session 1.1: a chatbot (ChatGPT, Claude, or Copilot Chat — whichever is on the Approved AI Tools List), a summarization tool, and one image generator (or OCR tool, if image gen is restricted by AUP).
- Activity worksheets (one per session, provided as printable PDFs and editable markdown).
- Whiteboard or large flip-chart for the closing share-out.

## Async fallback

For staff who cannot attend live:

- 20-minute recorded video per session.
- Guided worksheet matching the session activity.
- 5-question self-assessment quiz.
- Discussion forum or Teams channel for follow-up questions.

Async completion does not substitute for the closing 5-minute share-out. Async attendees should be paired with a Champion or supervisor for a 15-minute follow-up conversation within two weeks of completing the video.

## Common facilitation pitfalls

- **Too much "what is AI" content.** Attendees do not need a survey of machine learning history. They need to recognize a few categories of tools and start using them.
- **Demonstrating tools attendees can't use.** If the agency hasn't approved Tool X, don't demo Tool X. Demonstrate only what's on the Approved AI Tools List.
- **Pretending failure modes don't exist.** When the chatbot makes something up during the demo, name it. Hiding hallucinations destroys credibility faster than admitting them.
- **Skipping the closing share-out.** It feels like time pressure but it is the highest-leverage 5 minutes in the session.
