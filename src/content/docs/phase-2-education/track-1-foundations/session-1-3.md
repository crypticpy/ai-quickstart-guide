This is the Desire-stage session. Attendees move from "AI exists" to "AI might help me, specifically." The 90 minutes is structured around their actual jobs, not abstract capabilities.

## Learning objectives

By the end of this session, every attendee can:

1. Identify three tasks in their daily work where AI could provide assistance.
2. Articulate the human-in-the-loop principle — what AI does, what they still own.
3. Explain — to a peer or family member — what's changing about their role and what isn't.

## Audience

All staff. Pre-requisite: Session 1.1 (and ideally 1.2).

## Materials

- "My Job + AI" worksheet, one per attendee. Two columns: tasks I do (left), AI applicability rating + notes (right).
- Role evolution maps from the [Job-Impact Messaging Kit](/phase-2-education/job-impact-messaging/), printed and grouped by role family.
- Leadership commitment letter, in printed form. Read aloud at the start.
- One-pager on the human-in-the-loop principle (provided).

## 30-minute presentation

1. **Start by reading the leadership commitment letter aloud.** Don't summarize it. Read it. The signed version, on agency letterhead. This sets the frame for everything else in the session.
2. **Augmentation vs. automation, in one slide.** Augmentation means AI helps a human do work faster or better, with the human in the loop. Automation means AI does the work, with the human reviewing the output (or not). Most of what the agency is rolling out is augmentation. Headlines about "automation" usually describe augmentation.
3. **What stays human.** Judgment. Relationships. Accountability. Discretion. Empathy. Decisions that affect rights, benefits, or safety. AI does not own these — staff do. Slide deliberately spends 5 minutes here.
4. **What gets faster.** First-draft writing. Summarizing. Translating (with review). Searching across many documents. Extracting structured data from unstructured input. The 25–40% of work that is repetitive support-around-the-work, not the work.
5. **What becomes possible.** This is the Desire moment. With time freed by augmentation, what could you do that you've never had time to do well? Quality of relationships, follow-through, prevention, mentoring, learning. Use one or two examples from the role evolution maps.
6. **Walk through the role evolution map for the dominant role family in the room.** If the room is mixed, do two — but no more than two. Specifics over generality.

## 30-minute "My Job + AI" activity

Each attendee fills in the worksheet:

- **Left column.** List 5–8 tasks they did this week, or that they typically do in a week. Just the tasks, not how long they took.
- **Right column.** For each task, rate AI applicability: ⭐ (probably not), ⭐⭐ (maybe), ⭐⭐⭐ (definitely). Add a one-line note for any ⭐⭐⭐.

Then they switch with a partner. The partner reads the worksheet and asks one clarifying question per task. The point of the partner round is not feedback; it is articulation. Saying "I draft three emails like that a week" out loud is the moment a task becomes a candidate intake idea.

The facilitator circulates, listens, and prompts attendees who are stuck.

## 30-minute role-specific deep dive

Split the room into role-family groups. Up to 4 groups; if the room is too uniform, run as one large group. Each group has the role evolution map for their family on the table.

Each group works through three questions, capturing answers on a flip-chart:

1. **What stays the same in our roles?** (Builds shared confidence — confirms continuity.)
2. **What two or three tasks would we like to try AI on first?** (The ones from the My Job + AI sheet that came up multiple times.)
3. **What concerns do we want the Review Committee to hear?** (Surfaces real-world risk in language the committee will need.)

Each group reports out for 3 minutes. The facilitator captures the cross-cutting themes on the main board.

## Closing 5 minutes

Each attendee names one task — the most concrete one from their worksheet — that they will try with AI in the next two weeks. They also pick the one peer at the agency they will tell about their attempt. (Social commitment beats private intention.)

## Common questions and how to handle them

- **"This sounds great in theory but my actual work is different from these examples."** Acknowledge. Pull them aside after the session and walk through their My Job + AI sheet directly. The role maps are starting points; their job is the source of truth for them.
- **"What if I don't want to use AI?"** "That's OK. The agency is investing in tools because we think most staff will find at least one use that helps. If you don't, that's not a problem. We're not measuring AI use as a performance metric."
- **"Is this just a way to lay people off in 18 months?"** Refer back to the commitment letter (already read aloud at the start of session). Add: "Year 2 will be reviewed at Month 13 with the executive team and the union, with at least 60 days' notice of any change. We don't have a hidden plan."
- **"What about overload? If I save time, will I just be given more work?"** Honest answer: the agency's intent is that time goes to higher-quality work, not higher volume. But that depends on the manager. Track 7 (Middle Managers) addresses this directly. Encourage attendees to raise this in their next 1:1.

## Async fallback

- 18-minute recorded video, including the leadership commitment letter being read.
- "My Job + AI" worksheet completed independently.
- Reflection paragraph: _Which task on your worksheet are you most likely to try?_
- Optional 15-minute follow-up conversation with a Champion or supervisor.

## Evaluation

- **Level 2.** "Distinguish augmentation from automation" appears as a quiz question in Session 1.4.
- **Level 3.** Two-week follow-up email asks: did you try the task you committed to? Did you tell your peer? Target ≥60% yes on at least one of the two.
- **Level 4.** This session is a major driver of the intake submission rate. Track which sessions produce the highest follow-through.

## What to leave out

- Generic "AI will change the future of work" content.
- Specific salary or productivity claims for which the agency does not have its own data.
- Attempts to convince skeptics by quoting industry-wide AI productivity studies. Local examples beat global statistics.
