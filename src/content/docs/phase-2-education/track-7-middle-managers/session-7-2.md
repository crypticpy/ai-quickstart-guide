Session 7.1 prepared managers for the question every staff member will ask. Session 7.2 prepares them for what comes after — the slow, individual work of coaching adoption when staff respond differently. Some are eager. Some are skeptical. Some are anxious. Some quietly disengage. The manager's job is not to make every staff member adopt AI; it is to make experimentation safe enough that staff who would benefit can try without fear, and to honor the autonomy of those who don't.

## Learning objectives

By the end of this session, every manager can:

1. Recognize the four common resistance patterns and respond to each without escalating.
2. Apply the psychological-safety framework to AI experimentation in their own team.
3. Identify three concrete signals of healthy adoption — and three signals of unhealthy adoption — to watch for in the next 30 days.

## Audience and prerequisites

Same Track 7 audience. Session 7.1 should be complete.

## Materials

- Resistance pattern card set: skeptic, anxious, overenthusiast, passive (one card per pattern, with the recognition cues, the coaching response, and the miss-to-avoid).
- The Desire-stage table from the [Change Management Playbook](/phase-2-education/change-management/).
- The [Job-Impact Messaging Kit](/phase-2-education/job-impact-messaging/) talking-point cards.
- Facilitator guide with eight role-play vignettes (two per pattern).

## 30-minute discussion

### Topic 1 — What psychological safety means here (10 min)

Psychological safety is not "everyone agrees" or "no one is uncomfortable." It is the felt sense that **trying something and having it not work is not punished** — not socially, not in performance reviews, not in the next assignment. For AI specifically, this means:

- A staff member can try a tool, get a wrong answer, and say so out loud without being seen as a bad employee.
- A staff member can decline to use a tool without being seen as resistant or behind.
- A staff member can flag a concern about bias, privacy, or surveillance without being seen as a complainer.

The facilitator surfaces the room's own anxieties: where do you, as a manager, feel pressure to push adoption? Where do you feel pressure to demonstrate skepticism? Both pressures are real and both undermine the climate the team needs.

### Topic 2 — The four resistance patterns (15 min)

Walk through the patterns one at a time. For each, the facilitator describes:

- **Recognition cues** — what the manager hears or sees.
- **What the staff member actually needs** — usually different from what they appear to want.
- **The coaching response** — a specific opening sentence and a specific ask.
- **The miss to avoid** — the manager response that makes things worse.

#### Pattern 1 — The Skeptic

Cues: "AI is overhyped." "I've seen this kind of thing before." "It can't possibly do my job." Often expressed publicly, often the loudest voice in the room.

Need: To be taken seriously as a domain expert, not dismissed as a Luddite.

Coaching response: "You know this work better than anyone. I'd value your read on whether [specific tool] is useful for [specific task] — would you spend 20 minutes on it and tell me where it fails?" The skeptic is invited to evaluate, not to convert. Their judgment, applied to a bounded test, is exactly what the team needs.

Miss to avoid: arguing back, citing vendor demos, or implying their experience is outdated. The skeptic is often correct about specific failure modes; making them defend their position hardens it.

#### Pattern 2 — The Anxious

Cues: "I'm worried I'll lose my job." "I can't keep up with all this." Quieter than the skeptic. Often expressed in 1:1, not in groups.

Need: Reassurance grounded in the leadership commitment letter, plus a small concrete first step that won't fail.

Coaching response: "The commitment letter is real and I stand behind it — no AI-driven layoffs through [end date]. Your job has stayed in demand at this agency for 30 years and it will keep staying in demand. Want to try [low-stakes tool] together with me on [low-stakes task] this week?" The pairing matters — the manager doing it alongside the staff member is the highest-trust signal.

Miss to avoid: minimizing the concern ("don't worry about it") or piling on training resources ("here are 12 courses you can take"). Both raise anxiety.

#### Pattern 3 — The Overenthusiast

Cues: "I've already started using ChatGPT for everything." "I built a custom GPT for our intake process." Often a high performer, often well-intentioned.

Need: To be recognized for initiative AND redirected toward the approved path before they create a compliance issue.

Coaching response: "I love that you're trying things. I want you to keep that energy. Two requests: first, let's get [tool] reviewed through the intake form so you're protected if anything comes up; second, help me coach [skeptical or anxious peer] — they'd benefit from seeing your enthusiasm." The overenthusiast becomes a champion candidate (Track 5).

Miss to avoid: shutting them down ("stop using AI until we approve it"). The behavior continues but goes underground; now you have a shadow-AI problem instead of a coaching opportunity.

#### Pattern 4 — The Passive

Cues: nothing. They attend training, nod, and never use the tools. Don't push back, don't engage, just don't change.

Need: To be noticed without being pressured. Often the passive pattern hides one of the other three — usually anxious or skeptical — but the staff member doesn't trust the climate enough to surface it.

Coaching response: in a 1:1, "I noticed you haven't tried [tool] yet — no pressure either way, but I'm curious what's going on for you with it." Then listen. The opening lets the staff member name the underlying pattern, after which the coaching response for that pattern applies.

Miss to avoid: assuming compliance from silence ("they must be using it, they didn't say anything") or pressuring with metrics ("I need to see you using AI weekly"). Both produce false signal: the staff member learns to perform adoption without doing it.

### Topic 3 — Healthy vs. unhealthy adoption signals (5 min)

The room generates lists.

Healthy signals (target):

- Staff bring AI failures to 1:1 voluntarily.
- Staff say "I tried this and it was wrong" in team meetings without embarrassment.
- Staff submit intake-form ideas.
- Staff ask each other for tips ("how did you get a good output for X?").

Unhealthy signals (intervene):

- Adoption-rate-by-headcount climbs but quality of submitted work plateaus or drops.
- Staff stop talking about AI in meetings (silence is not consensus).
- A few high performers handle all AI work; everyone else is unchanged.
- Staff hide AI use from the manager (shadow AI).

The unhealthy signals are the ones managers miss most. Healthy adoption is noisy; unhealthy adoption is quiet.

## 30-minute scenario practice

Eight vignettes, two per resistance pattern. Pairs work three vignettes (selected by the facilitator to span all four patterns), 7 minutes each (5 min role-play, 2 min debrief). Switch roles between vignettes.

Sample vignettes the facilitator can use:

- **Skeptic.** "I'm a 22-year case worker. The chatbot got the SNAP eligibility cut-off wrong twice last week. We're not putting that in front of clients." Manager's job: invite the skeptic to bound-test a specific tool on a specific task.
- **Skeptic.** "I read about that hospital that paid $500K to settle a discrimination case from a hiring AI. Why are we walking into this?" Manager's job: validate the concern, point to the Review Committee and Tier-3 process, ask the staff member to be on the committee.
- **Anxious.** "My kid is in college for IT and they say AI is going to replace half the office in five years. Should I be looking at jobs?" Manager's job: ground the conversation in the commitment letter and offer a low-stakes pairing.
- **Anxious.** "I'm 58. I'm not learning a whole new way of working at this point in my career." Manager's job: validate, point to the role evolution map, offer a 20-minute pairing on one small task.
- **Overenthusiast.** "I've been running our weekly status report through Claude on my personal account. It's so much faster — I should show everyone." Manager's job: appreciate, redirect to approved tools and intake form, recruit as champion.
- **Overenthusiast.** "I built a custom prompt that classifies intake requests automatically. I want to deploy it for the whole division next week." Manager's job: appreciate, slow it down, explain that division-scale deployment goes through Review Committee, position as Tier-2 candidate.
- **Passive.** Staff member who has attended Track 1 but, six weeks later, has not tried any tool. Says "yeah, sure, I'll get to it" when asked. Manager's job: open the door without pressure, listen for the underlying pattern.
- **Passive.** Staff member nods through every meeting and submits no intake ideas. Manager's job: 1:1 inquiry, "I'd love to hear what you're noticing — even what's not working."

After all rounds, full-room debrief: which pattern was hardest? Which response felt least natural? What would you do differently?

## 15-minute action planning

Each manager:

1. Names one staff member who fits each pattern (or "n/a" if none).
2. Picks the pattern they will work on first in the next 30 days.
3. Writes the opening sentence for the coaching conversation.
4. Schedules a 30-day check-in with their peer manager from Session 7.1.

The 30-day target: every manager has had the coaching conversation with the named staff member for their chosen pattern.

## Common questions managers ask

- **"What if a staff member fits two patterns at once?"** Common. Anxious skeptics, passive overenthusiasts. Coach the more dominant pattern first; the other often resolves once the first is addressed.
- **"What if I'm the resistance pattern myself?"** Honest answer: you might be. The Champions network and peer-manager debriefs exist precisely so managers can name their own patterns and coach each other through them. Don't hide it from your peer.
- **"What if I'm pressured by my own boss to push adoption faster?"** Surface it to the AI program lead. Pressure that contradicts the leadership commitment letter is a governance problem, not a manager problem. Don't transmit the pressure to your team.
- **"What if a staff member's resistance is correct?"** Sometimes it is. The skeptic who says "this tool is wrong for this task" is often right. Take the input seriously, document it, and feed it back to the Review Committee or the AI program lead. Correct resistance improves the program; suppressed correct resistance damages it.

## Async fallback

- Recorded discussion module covering psychological safety and the four patterns.
- Self-paced scenario practice with paired manager.
- Resistance pattern card set, printed.
- A 30-day reflection submitted to the AI program lead: which pattern showed up on your team, what you tried, what worked or didn't.

## Evaluation

- **Level 2.** End-of-session 5-question knowledge check covering the four patterns and their coaching responses.
- **Level 3.** 30-day follow-up: managers report which patterns showed up, what coaching responses they tried, and what they observed.
- **Level 3.** Healthy-signal tracking: do staff in this team voluntarily bring AI failures into 1:1s? Asked at 90 days.
- **Level 4.** Adoption breadth metric: percentage of staff in trained-manager teams who have submitted at least one intake-form idea, vs. control teams. Target 2× lift.
