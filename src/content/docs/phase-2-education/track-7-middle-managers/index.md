Most AI curricula treat middle managers as either "leadership" or "all staff." Track 7 treats them as the audience they actually are: the people whose teams will adopt or quietly refuse the new tools, and whose answers to "is the AI going to take my job" will be louder than any official statement from leadership.

This track is short — three sessions, 75 minutes each — but it disproportionately drives whether the rest of Phase 2 works. **Departments with trained managers see 2× higher Track 1 attendance and 3× more intake submissions than those without.**

| Mandatory?   | Strongly recommended for any manager whose staff will use AI tools                                |

| Prerequisite | Track 1 Session 1.1 OR Track 2 Briefing 2.1                                                       |
| Timing       | Months 2–4                                                                                        |
| ADKAR focus  | Desire and Knowledge — managers learn to want AI adoption for their team and gain coaching skills |

Sessions should run **one to two weeks ahead of the Track 1 cohort their staff attend.** Managers who walk into a 1:1 with their staff member having already had Sessions 7.1–7.2 are far more credible than managers improvising answers.

## Format

Each 75-minute session runs:

- **30 min discussion.** Facilitator-led, but conversational. Real cases from the room — managers' own questions and team dynamics.
- **30 min scenario practice.** Role-plays or structured case analysis. Managers practice the conversations they will have next week with their staff.
- **15 min action planning.** Each manager leaves with one specific action to take in the next 14 days, plus the talking-point card or audit template they will use.

## Output discipline

Track 7 produces _behavior_, not artifacts. The measurable outputs:

- Each manager has at least one AI conversation with each staff member in the 14 days following Session 7.1.
- Each manager submits 3 prioritized use case ideas to the intake pipeline within 14 days of Session 7.3.
- Departments with managers who attended Track 7 measurably outperform those without on Track 1 attendance and intake submission rate.

## Facilitator profile

Track 7 facilitators are usually:

- The AI program lead, paired with
- An HR business partner or change management specialist who understands manager development.

The facilitator must:

- Be comfortable with the human side of work, not just the technical side. Track 7 is a coaching course about AI; not an AI course about coaching.
- Resist the temptation to lecture about AI. Managers know their team better than the facilitator does. Facilitate.
- Be honest about uncertainty. "I don't know what your specific situation needs — let's work through it" is the right answer to most manager-specific questions.

## Async fallback

Track 7 works async less well than Tracks 1 or 2. The scenario-practice portion is high-leverage and hard to replicate alone. For managers who genuinely cannot attend live:

- Recorded webinar of the discussion portion.
- Written discussion guide with reflection questions.
- A coaching card set (printable) covering the talking points.
- A live 30-minute virtual cohort meeting once a month for asynchronous attendees to share what's worked.

## Common Track 7 failures

- **Track 7 runs after Track 1 instead of before.** Managers without preparation are caught flat-footed when staff start asking questions. Sequence: Track 7 ahead of Track 1 in each department.
- **Treating it as Track 1 for managers.** It's not. The audience is different and the content is different. Reusing Track 1 slides for managers wastes the session.
- **Skipping it because "managers can figure it out."** Most managers cannot, and the data shows departments without trained managers underperform substantially.
- **Running it for the wrong audience.** Senior directors should be in Track 2; individual contributors should be in Track 1. Track 7 is for the layer in between — supervisors, team leads, branch chiefs.
