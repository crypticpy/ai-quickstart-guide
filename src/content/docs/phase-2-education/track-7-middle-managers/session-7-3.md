The first two Track 7 sessions equip managers to lead conversations about AI. Session 7.3 equips them to find, prioritize, and submit the workflows where AI would actually help their team. The output is concrete: three intake submissions per manager, in the form, with the manager's name as sponsor. The intake pipeline is the load-bearing input to Phases 5 and 6 — without manager-sourced ideas, the pipeline starves and the platform builds the wrong things.

## Learning objectives

By the end of this session, every manager can:

1. Use the workflow audit template to surface candidate AI-augmentation tasks in their unit.
2. Apply the prioritization framework (frequency × pain × suitability) to rank candidates.
3. Submit three prioritized candidates to the intake form with sufficient context for Review Committee triage.

## Audience and prerequisites

Same Track 7 audience. Sessions 7.1 and 7.2 should be complete. Each manager comes prepared to discuss their team's actual work — bring real examples, not hypotheticals.

## Materials

- The workflow audit template (one page).
- The [AI Use Case Intake Form](/phase-2-education/use-case-intake/), open and projected.
- The risk classification matrix from [Phase 1](/phase-1-governance/risk-classification/).
- The augmentation candidate list from Session 7.1's Topic 2 (if managers attended together).
- Printed examples: three completed intake submissions from peer agencies (anonymized) showing what good looks like.

## 30-minute discussion

### Topic 1 — What makes a good AI candidate (10 min)

Three filters. A workflow is a strong AI candidate when all three are true:

1. **It happens often.** Daily, weekly, monthly — not the once-a-year strategic plan. Volume creates ROI; one-off tasks rarely justify the integration cost.
2. **It is painful.** Either time-intensive (staff complain about it), error-prone (mistakes have consequences), or boring (it's the work staff put off). Pain is the team's signal that automation has value.
3. **It is suitable.** AI is good at: pattern matching across documents, drafting first versions of structured text, summarizing large inputs, classifying or routing, extracting structured data from unstructured sources. AI is bad at: making consequential decisions about specific individuals, accessing live authoritative data without grounding, replacing judgment in ambiguous cases.

A workflow that is frequent + painful + suitable is a prime candidate. Frequent + painful + unsuitable is a wishlist item — important to capture but not a near-term project. Suitable + infrequent is a "maybe later." All three filters matter.

### Topic 2 — Walk through the audit template (10 min)

The audit template asks managers to list, for their unit:

- Top 5 recurring tasks by time spent (gut estimate, not precision).
- Top 3 tasks staff complain about most.
- Top 3 tasks where errors have downstream consequences.
- Top 3 tasks that involve reading or writing structured/unstructured text at volume.

Overlap is the goal. A task that appears on three of those lists is a prime candidate. The facilitator works one example through publicly so managers see how it goes.

Worked example: "Drafting initial responses to constituent inquiry emails." Time: ~6 hours/week per case worker. Complained about: yes, "the same questions over and over." Errors: occasional, "wrong policy citation" → constituent gets wrong information. Text-heavy: yes, both reading and writing. → Strong candidate.

### Topic 3 — Prioritization and the intake form's expectations (10 min)

The intake form needs:

- The unit and the manager (sponsor).
- The task or workflow in plain English.
- The current manual baseline — time per instance, volume per week, who does it.
- Why it matters — what gets better if it's faster or more accurate.
- What data is involved and where it lives.
- Provisional tier (the form auto-classifies, but manager judgment matters).

Managers should not over-engineer the submission. The Review Committee and AI program lead refine the scope. The manager's job is to surface it, attach baseline numbers, and put their name on it.

## 30-minute working session

### Block A — Solo audit (15 min)

Each manager works the audit template for their own unit. Silent work — managers reflect on their team's actual workload, not what they wish it were. The facilitator circulates and asks clarifying questions ("how often does that happen?", "who currently does that?").

The output: each manager has a draft list of 5-10 candidate tasks with rough notes on frequency, pain, and suitability.

### Block B — Pair triage (10 min)

Managers pair with the same partner from Sessions 7.1 and 7.2 (if possible). Each manager presents their candidate list in 4 minutes, partner asks questions in 1 minute, then switch.

Partner's job: stress-test the candidates. Is this really weekly? Is the pain real or assumed? Would a staff member from this team agree with the suitability call? The pairing surfaces the candidates that don't survive scrutiny.

The output: each manager has the same list, narrowed and improved by partner pushback.

### Block C — Submit three (5 min)

Each manager picks the top three candidates and submits them to the intake form, live, in the room. The facilitator opens the form on the projector and walks through the first submission together if the room hasn't used the form before. Subsequent submissions are silent solo work — managers fill the form on their laptop.

The intake form auto-classifies tier and exports a markdown record. The manager sends the export to the AI program lead's intake inbox. By the end of Block C, the inbox has 3N submissions for the N managers in the room.

## 15-minute action planning

Each manager:

1. Confirms the three submissions are in the inbox.
2. Names the staff member whose work is most affected by the top-priority candidate.
3. Schedules a conversation with that staff member to walk through the candidate together — staff input refines the scope before Review Committee triage.
4. Schedules a 90-day reflection with their peer manager: which submissions advanced, which stalled, what was learned.

The 14-day target: all submissions are in the inbox and acknowledged by the AI program lead.

## After Session 7.3

Track 7 ends here as a structured course, but the work continues. The AI program lead processes the intake submissions in the standard cadence:

- Triage (within 7 days). Tier classification confirmed; obvious blockers flagged; submissions grouped into clusters where multiple managers requested similar things.
- Review Committee discussion (next standing meeting). Tier-2 candidates get pilot scoping; Tier-3 candidates get bias-review track assigned.
- Manager feedback loop (within 30 days). Each manager receives a status update on each submission, even if the answer is "not yet" or "merged with another submission."

The feedback loop matters. Managers who submit and then hear nothing stop submitting. A 30-day status update is a strong default for the AI program lead; agencies with a different review cadence should publish their local response target and meet it consistently.

## Common questions managers ask

- **"What if all my best candidates are Tier-3?"** Possible — some teams handle high-stakes decisions almost exclusively. Submit them anyway; the Review Committee triages. Tier-3 candidates take longer but are worth surfacing early because they need bias review and contestation pathways before pilots.
- **"What if my staff disagree with my prioritization?"** Listen to them. The follow-up conversation in action-planning step 3 exists for this. If staff have a different read, revise the submission or submit a fourth candidate the staff member proposed.
- **"What if I submit something and the answer is no?"** "No" is a useful answer. The Review Committee documents the reason — usually unsuitability, vendor gap, or lower priority than other candidates — and the manager can resubmit when conditions change. The committee tracks "no, not yet" candidates separately.
- **"What if my unit's work isn't a good AI fit at all?"** Possible. Some workflows are inherently judgment-heavy or relationship-heavy. Submitting "we audited and found nothing suitable" is a valid output and is recorded as such. Don't manufacture candidates.

## Async fallback

- Recorded discussion module on the audit framework.
- Self-paced workflow audit using the template.
- Submitted via the intake form within 14 days of starting async.
- 30-minute virtual peer pairing scheduled by the AI program lead.

## Evaluation

- **Level 2.** End-of-session check: each manager submits three intake forms within the session.
- **Level 3.** 30-day check: percentage of submissions that reached Review Committee triage. Target 100%.
- **Level 3.** 90-day check: percentage of submissions that advanced to a Tier-2 pilot or Tier-3 review. Target 30%+ (some submissions are intentionally deprioritized).
- **Level 4.** Pipeline health: trained-manager departments produce ≥3× more intake submissions than untrained departments.
