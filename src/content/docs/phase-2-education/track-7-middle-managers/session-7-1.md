The first manager session is about the conversation managers will have over and over for the next 12 months: a staff member, alone in a 1:1 or in a hallway, asking some version of _"is the AI going to take my job?"_ Managers who walk in unprepared improvise — and improvised answers from supervisors are read as agency policy. Session 7.1 prevents that.

## Learning objectives

By the end of this session, every manager can:

1. Distinguish augmentation from automation for their specific team's roles.
2. Identify three tasks in their unit that AI could augment.
3. Articulate the leadership commitment letter in their own words, without reading it.

## Audience and prerequisites

Division managers, team leads, branch chiefs, supervisors. Track 1 Session 1.1 OR Track 2 Briefing 2.1 required as prerequisite.

## Materials

- The signed leadership commitment letter — every manager has a copy.
- Role evolution maps from the [Job-Impact Messaging Kit](/phase-2-education/job-impact-messaging/), grouped by role family.
- The talking-point card set (printable, 4 cards): "Will AI take my job?", "I tried it once and it was wrong", "I don't want to", "I'm worried about [bias / privacy]".
- One-page facilitator guide with the four scenarios.

## 30-minute discussion

### Topic 1 — The leadership commitment, read aloud (5 min)

Start the same way Track 1 Session 1.3 starts: read the commitment letter aloud, in full, on letterhead. The same words their staff will hear in Track 1.

This isn't decoration. Managers cannot speak to the commitment with credibility unless they have heard it in the same form their staff will hear it. They must be able to repeat it without paraphrasing.

### Topic 2 — Augmentation vs. automation, applied to your team (10 min)

Managers split into pairs by role family (or stay together if the room is uniform). Each pair lists:

- 3 things on their team's plate that AI could probably help with (augmentation).
- 1 thing AI is being pitched as automating that they're skeptical of.
- 2 things on their team's plate that AI absolutely cannot do (judgment, relationship, accountability).

The facilitator captures the augmentation list on the board — many of these are candidate intake submissions for Session 7.3.

### Topic 3 — The role evolution map for your role family (15 min)

Walk through the role evolution map for each represented role family in the room. The Job-Impact Kit provides maps for caseworkers, inspectors, analysts, customer service, IT staff. For role families not covered, build one in the room — same format (what stays, what gets faster, what becomes possible, new skills required).

The output is each manager has a role-specific page they can hand a staff member who asks "what's changing for me." Generic maps don't work — staff need to see their role.

## 30-minute scenario practice

Four scenarios, run in pairs (one manager-role, one staff-role; switch halfway). Each scenario has 7 minutes for the practice, 1 minute for partner feedback, then switch.

### Scenario 1 — "Is AI going to take my job?"

The staff member asks directly. The manager:

- Refers to the commitment letter (date, scope, "no AI-driven layoffs through [end date]").
- Names the role evolution: what stays, what gets faster, what becomes possible.
- Offers to walk through the role-specific map at the next 1:1.

The talking-point card from the [Job-Impact Messaging Kit](/phase-2-education/job-impact-messaging/) is the source script. Practice until it sounds like the manager, not the script.

### Scenario 2 — "I tried it once and it was wrong"

The staff member tried the chatbot, got a hallucinated answer, and is now skeptical. The manager:

- Validates: yes, AI does that, and recognizing it is part of the skill.
- Refers to Track 6 Domain Lab on "Is This AI Output Correct?".
- Asks the staff member to try one more thing on lower-stakes work and report back.

The miss to avoid: "well, you used the wrong tool." Defensiveness destroys trust.

### Scenario 3 — "I don't want to use AI"

The staff member is direct: I'm fine with the way I work. The manager:

- Confirms there's no performance penalty for not using AI.
- Asks the staff member to try one specific low-stakes use once. If it doesn't help, fine.
- Doesn't argue. Honors the staff member's autonomy.

The miss to avoid: trying to convince. AI adoption is a Desire-stage challenge, not a Knowledge problem; persuasion in 1:1 doesn't move it.

### Scenario 4 — "I'm worried about [bias / privacy / surveillance]"

The staff member raises a substantive concern. The manager:

- Validates it as a real concern that the agency takes seriously.
- Refers to the Review Committee's role and the equity officer / privacy officer / ombuds.
- Asks if the staff member wants to be on the committee or contribute to a Tier-3 review.

The miss to avoid: dismissing the concern as fear. Some staff have correct objections and the agency benefits from hearing them.

## 15-minute action planning

Each manager:

1. Names the staff member they will have the AI conversation with first.
2. Picks the talking-point card most likely to come up.
3. Schedules the 1:1 in the next 7 days.
4. Identifies one peer manager to debrief with after.

The 14-day target: every manager has had at least one AI conversation with each staff member.

## Common questions managers ask

- **"What if I'm asked something I don't know?"** "Let me find out and follow up by [date]." This is the right answer for any manager on any topic. Then actually follow up.
- **"What if leadership changes the policy mid-year?"** Refer to the commitment letter's review clause: any change is announced with at least 60 days' notice. If something changes, you'll know with time to talk to your team.
- **"What about the staff who quietly sandbag?"** Don't escalate. Have one direct conversation, document it, and let it ride. Quiet refusal is a stage in adoption; rarely a personnel problem on its own.
- **"What if my staff are using consumer AI tools against the AUP?"** Address it as a coaching conversation first, not a discipline conversation. Most staff using shadow AI are doing it because the approved tools aren't useful enough yet, not because they're malicious. Coach toward the approved list; if the approved list has a gap, that's intake-form fodder.

## Async fallback

- Recorded 30-minute discussion module.
- Self-paced scenario practice with paired manager (find someone in your cohort).
- Talking-point card set, printed and laminated.
- Within two weeks of completing async, manager submits a 3-question check-in: which scenarios did you practice? which conversation did you have with staff? what came up that you didn't expect?

## Evaluation

- **Level 2.** End-of-session 5-question knowledge check covering the commitment letter scope, augmentation vs. automation, and the four scenarios.
- **Level 3.** Two-week follow-up: managers report whether they had the AI conversation with each direct report, and what came up.
- **Level 4.** Track 1 attendance rate in their department vs. departments without trained managers. Target 2× lift.
