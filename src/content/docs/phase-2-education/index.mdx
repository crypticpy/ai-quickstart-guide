Phase 1 gives your agency the policies. Phase 2 gives your agency the **people who will use them**. It runs in parallel with every other phase: training kicks off Month 1 and never really ends — by design.

The most expensive AI failure is not a technical one. It is buying a tool nobody uses, or a tool everyone uses badly. Both are training failures, not technology failures. This phase prevents them.

## What you will accomplish

- A shared vocabulary across staff, managers, IT, and leadership — so a "Tier 2 use case" or "RAG over policy docs" means the same thing in HR, in Public Works, and in the City Manager's office.
- A working **intake pipeline**: any staff member can submit an AI use case idea in under five minutes, and your Review Committee has a queue to triage.
- Manager confidence. Supervisors are the single biggest predictor of AI adoption in their unit — and the most overlooked audience in nearly every public-sector AI training program. Phase 2 fixes that.
- Sustained momentum past Month 6, when the launch energy fades and most training programs quietly die.

Tracks are organized by **audience role**, not technical depth. Following the U.S. Department of Labor AI Literacy Framework (February 2026), every staff member fits into exactly one primary track and may participate in others as relevant. No one is left out, and no one is in the wrong room.

## Why "Middle Manager Enablement" exists as its own track

Most AI curricula collapse middle managers into either "leadership" or "all staff." Both are wrong.

- They are not directors. They cannot approve budget or set strategy.
- They are not individual contributors. They are the people whose teams will adopt or quietly refuse the new tools.
- They are the people whose staff ask "is the AI going to take my job" — and they need a real answer, not a generic talking point.

Track 7 is short (three sessions) but it disproportionately drives whether the rest of the curriculum works. Departments with trained managers see 2× higher Track 1 attendance and 3× more intake submissions than those without. Skip it, and the rest of Phase 2 underperforms.

## Change model: ADKAR + Kirkpatrick

Every track follows the **ADKAR** sequence (Awareness → Desire → Knowledge → Ability → Reinforcement) and is measured using the **Kirkpatrick** four levels (Reaction, Learning, Behavior, Results). Both models are decades old, well-validated in public-sector training, and require no proprietary licensing.

The [Change Management Playbook](/phase-2-education/change-management/) is the operational guide for facilitators. It maps each session to its ADKAR stage, names the resistance patterns to expect, and gives concrete language for managers and trainers.

## Honest about job impact

Employee anxiety about AI replacing their jobs is the single biggest barrier to training adoption. The curriculum addresses this directly — not implicitly through ADKAR, not through vague reassurance from Comms.

The [Job-Impact Messaging Kit](/phase-2-education/job-impact-messaging/) provides:

- A leadership commitment template (no AI-driven layoffs during the 12-month program)
- Role evolution maps for the major job families
- Talking points for managers facing "is AI going to take my job" conversations
- Union/labor engagement guidance for organized agencies

If you cannot ship the leadership commitment, do not start Phase 2. The training will be received as cover for layoffs and you will burn trust you cannot rebuild.

## Sustainability past Month 6

The hardest part of training is not Month 1. It is Month 7. By then the launch crowd has moved on, the easy tools are mostly used by the early adopters, and managers have stopped asking about AI in their 1:1s. Without active reinforcement, training programs fade and the intake pipeline dries up.

The [Sustainability Playbook](/phase-2-education/sustainability/) defines the mechanisms that keep momentum going through Month 12 and into Year 2: monthly Lunch & Learns embedded in existing meetings, peer storytelling, pioneer recognition, and a quarterly engagement pulse that triggers a pause if scores drop.

## The intake form is the connective tissue

Track 1 ends with every attendee submitting a real use case via the [AI Use Case Intake Form](/phase-2-education/use-case-intake/). That form is the single mechanism that connects training to action:

- Staff submit ideas → Review Committee classifies tier → high-value Tier 1 / Tier 2 ideas get scheduled → some become starter projects in Phase 6.
- Champions (Track 5) review the queue monthly and provide peer feedback.
- Managers (Track 7) coach their teams to submit ideas, not just tolerate AI.
- Domain Labs (Track 6) often emerge from intake patterns ("we keep getting summarization requests — let's run a Domain Lab on it").

Without the intake form, training is a one-way push. With it, training becomes a two-way pipeline.

## Async and self-paced delivery

Reality: 30–40% of content will be consumed asynchronously. Every track has a self-paced fallback so agencies that cannot pull staff offline still participate.

| Track                | Async format                                                            | Support channel                       |
| -------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------- |
| Track 1: Foundations | Recorded video (20 min) + guided worksheet + quiz per session           | Discussion forum or Teams channel     |
| Track 2: Leadership  | 1-page brief + 10-min video + decision prompt card per briefing         | Office hours (monthly 30-min drop-in) |
| Track 3: Governance  | Template + walkthrough video + self-paced completion guide per artifact | Peer review partner (paired agencies) |
| Track 4: Developers  | Step-by-step lab guide + starter code + solution code + recorded demo   | Slack/Discord developer channel       |
| Track 5: Champions   | Async check-in survey + shared wins board + monthly digest email        | Always-on Teams/Slack channel         |
| Track 6: Domain Labs | 5-min demo video + try-it-yourself card + reflection question           | Embedded in department communication  |
| Track 7: Managers    | Recorded webinar + discussion guide + coaching card set                 | Manager peer cohort (monthly virtual) |

Live > async whenever possible — the social pressure of a session you sit in builds adoption faster than a video. But async beats nothing, and nothing is what most agencies actually deliver in Year 1.

## Plain-English Guide to Phase 2 Terms

- **ADKAR.** Awareness, Desire, Knowledge, Ability, Reinforcement — Prosci's change-management sequence used to predict and address resistance during a rollout.
- **Kirkpatrick.** A four-level training evaluation model: Reaction (did learners like it), Learning (did they learn), Behavior (did behavior change), Results (did outcomes change).
- **Track.** A learning path for one audience role. Phase 2 has seven tracks; every staff member fits into exactly one primary track.
- **Champions Network.** Cross-department peer advocates who sustain momentum between formal training events and surface emerging use cases from their units.
- **Intake pipeline.** The form plus triage process that turns staff use case ideas into prioritized projects routed to the Review Committee.
- **Change model.** The framework used to predict and address resistance during a rollout. Phase 2 uses ADKAR for the sequence and Kirkpatrick for the measurement.
- **Async delivery.** Self-paced video plus worksheet alternative for staff who cannot attend live sessions. Live is preferred; async beats nothing.

## Research basis

U.S. Department of Labor AI Literacy Framework (February 2026), Prosci ADKAR change-management model (Hiatt 2006), Kirkpatrick four-level evaluation (Kirkpatrick & Kirkpatrick 2016), GovEx AI for City Government curriculum, Austin APH "Ask About AI" series (sustaining 70–100 participants per session), and the SPACE framework for developer experience (informing Track 4 measurement).

## Off-ramp

A complete and valuable Phase 2 stop point: **Tracks 1, 3, and 7 delivered, intake form live, leadership commitment statement signed, and a quarterly engagement pulse running.** That alone delivers most of the cultural risk-reduction benefit. Tracks 4, 5, 6 are accelerators — important but not blocking.
