Track 2 is for the people who control budget, hiring, and political risk. Their time is measured in 15-minute blocks, their patience for vendor pitches is exhausted, and their AI exposure is mostly headlines and the occasional polished demo. Track 2 corrects for this.

Each briefing is **60 minutes**, not 90. The format is high-signal: 30 minutes of substance, 20 minutes of structured discussion, 10 minutes for the explicit decision prompt that closes the session. No hands-on activity, no role-plays — that work happens in Tracks 1 and 7.

## Format

Each briefing is structured 30 / 20 / 10:

- **30 min substance.** Slide-supported but conversational. Three or four key decisions to surface, in plain language. No technical jargon unless explicitly defined. Cite specific recent examples (case studies, peer-jurisdiction outcomes, statutes). Vague language signals weak preparation.
- **20 min structured discussion.** Two or three discussion prompts the facilitator brings. The room talks about the decisions, not the technology.
- **10 min decision close.** The decision prompt is named, the decision is made (or deferred to a named follow-up), and the resolution is captured in writing in the meeting record.

The key discipline: **every briefing closes with a decision in writing.** "We talked about it" is a failure mode.

## Output per briefing

Each briefing produces a one-page **takeaway memo**, distributed within 48 hours, signed by the executive who chaired it. The memo includes:

- The decision (or named follow-up date if deferred).
- The two or three key facts that drove the decision.
- The named follow-up actions and owners.

These memos accumulate into a **decision history** that the AI program lead references for the rest of the year. When a department director asks "did we already decide this?" — the memo set is the answer.

## Facilitator profile

The Track 2 facilitator is typically the AI program lead, sometimes paired with the CIO or an external advisor. The facilitator:

- Has read the relevant Phase 1 governance pages cold and can cite specific clauses by reference, not paraphrase.
- Brings two or three concrete recommendations to each briefing — not "here are the options," but "here is what I recommend, and here's the alternative I rejected."
- Is willing to disagree with the room. Executives detect performative facilitation immediately.

## Async fallback

For executives who cannot attend live:

- 1-page executive brief per briefing (single page, no exceptions).
- 10-minute recorded video covering the substance.
- Decision prompt card delivered by email after the video.
- Monthly 30-minute office hour with the AI program lead for follow-up.

Async is acceptable for executives but always less effective than live. Push for live attendance — even one or two per briefing — and accept async only as a fallback for genuine schedule constraints.

## Common Track 2 failures

- **The AI program lead gives a Track 1 presentation.** Executives don't need vocabulary; they need decisions framed. Repurposing Track 1 slides destroys the briefing's value.
- **Every briefing ends with "we'll think about it."** No decision means no progress. If the discussion isn't ready for a decision, name the specific information needed and the date by which the decision will be made.
- **Vendors are invited.** Track 2 briefings are agency-only. Vendor pitches happen elsewhere. Mixing the two compromises the discussion.
- **The same slide deck for every executive team.** Each agency's politics, statutory context, and budget posture is different. Customize.
