The most important conversation in your AI rollout is the one staff are having with each other in the parking lot, not the one in your training room. They are asking each other "is the AI going to take my job?" — and they are answering each other based on what they read on social media because nobody at the agency has given them a straight answer.

This page is the straight answer kit.

## Strongly recommended: a leadership commitment letter

Before broad Phase 2 training runs, the executive sponsor (Director, City Manager, CIO, or equivalent) should sign a written letter committing to:

1. **No AI-driven layoffs** during the 12-month program.
2. **Time freed by AI augmentation goes to higher-value work, not headcount reduction.**
3. **Staff displaced by automation will be retrained and reassigned**, not separated, for the duration of the program.
4. **Annual review.** The commitment is reviewed at Month 13 with the executive team and the union (if applicable). Any change to the commitment is announced with at least 60 days' notice.

If you cannot get this letter signed, do not launch broad staff training as if job-impact concerns are resolved. Scope down, run a listening session, or start with a department-level pilot where the sponsor can make a clear commitment. Without some visible commitment, training is easy for staff to read as cover for layoffs.

The letter is not a press release. It is a one-page memo, on agency letterhead, distributed in Track 1 Session 1.1, posted on the agency intranet, and emailed by the executive sponsor (not Comms, not HR — the executive sponsor personally) to all staff in the program scope.

For an all-staff launch conversation, use the [Leadership Commitment and Staff Launch deck source](/deck-sources/program/leadership-commitment-staff-launch.md) as a starting point, then replace the placeholders with approved local commitment language.

## How this page scales for your agency

| Path | Minimum useful version | Add when capacity exists |
| ---- | ---------------------- | ------------------------ |
| Small / no dedicated IT | Signed note from the department head, short FAQ, one manager talking-points card | Role maps for the two or three largest job families |
| Medium | Executive letter, role maps for major job families, manager talking points, labor briefing if applicable | Pulse survey and quarterly update from the sponsor |
| Large / federated | Executive letter plus department-level supplements, labor and HR coordination, full role-map library | Bargaining-cycle language, formal change network, recurring public updates |

> **TO:** All [Agency] Staff
> **FROM:** [Director / City Manager / CIO Name]
> **DATE:** [Effective Date]
> **RE:** Our Commitment to Staff During the AI Program
>
> Over the next twelve months, [Agency] is investing in AI tools to help staff do their jobs better — not to replace them. I want to be direct about what this means for you.
>
> **No layoffs driven by AI during this program.** From [Start Date] through [End Date], no employee will be separated from [Agency] because their work has been automated. If a workflow changes substantially because of AI, the staff doing that work will be retrained and reassigned, with full pay and benefits.
>
> **Time savings benefit you.** When AI helps you finish a task faster, that time is yours to spend on higher-value work — work that needs human judgment, that builds relationships with the public we serve, or that you simply have not had time to do well. We are not measuring AI's success in headcount; we are measuring it in service quality.
>
> **What I expect of you.** Try the tools. Tell us when they don't work. Submit ideas for where they could help. We will listen, and we will move ideas forward when they make sense.
>
> **What you can expect of me.** I will report on how AI is being used at [Agency] every quarter. I will name the wins and the failures publicly. If we discover that an AI use is causing harm, we will pause it. If you have concerns, the [Equity Officer / Ombuds / HR Director] will hear them confidentially.
>
> This program is built on your trust. I take that seriously.
>
> [Signature]
> [Name, Title]

### What to leave out

- **Vague language about "the future of work."** It comes off as evasive. Specifics build trust; abstractions break it.
- **Mention of competitive pressure or "or we'll fall behind."** This sounds like the precursor to layoffs, even when it isn't.
- **Promises beyond the 12-month window.** A real one-year commitment is more credible than a vague "as long as possible." Year 2 will be addressed at the Month 13 review.

## Role evolution maps

For each major job family in scope, prepare a one-page "before and after" showing how AI changes the role. Use the format below. Keep it specific to the role. Generic maps don't work — staff need to see _their_ job.

### Format

Each map has four sections:

1. **What stays the same.** The judgment, the relationships, the accountability — these don't move.
2. **What gets faster.** The repetitive drafting, summarization, lookup, or extraction work that staff already recognize as paperwork-around-the-work.
3. **What becomes possible.** The work staff didn't have time to do well. This is where the time savings go.
4. **New skills required.** What staff will learn during the program. Pair with the relevant Track 1, 6, or 7 content.

### Example: Caseworker (Health & Human Services)

**What stays the same.** You still own the case. You still meet with families. You still make eligibility determinations and handle appeals. AI has no role in deciding who is eligible — that is your job, and it is bound by statute, training, and your professional judgment.

**What gets faster.** Drafting routine correspondence. Summarizing case files at intake. Translating documents for non-English-speaking clients (with human review). Extracting structured information from intake forms and uploaded documents. The tedious caseload time that is paperwork-around-the-work, not the work.

**What becomes possible.** More time with families per case. Earlier follow-up on at-risk cases. Catching patterns across your caseload (recurring service gaps) that you couldn't see when buried in paperwork. The kind of preventive, relational work that case management was supposed to be when the agency was founded.

**New skills required.** Track 1 (4 sessions). Track 6 micro-labs on summarization, drafting, and policy lookup. One-on-one with your supervisor to map your specific workflow.

### Example: Inspector (Code Enforcement / Public Health / Environmental)

**What stays the same.** You still walk the site. You still make the call on whether something is a violation. You still talk to the property owner or operator. AI does not visit sites, does not exercise enforcement discretion, does not testify in hearings.

**What gets faster.** Looking up the relevant code section in the field. Drafting standard violation notices. Generating photo captions. Summarizing the inspection history of a property before a follow-up visit. Pulling case file data for prosecutorial referral.

**What becomes possible.** More inspections per week. Earlier proactive enforcement on patterns (one operator, multiple sites, escalating violations). Time to write better case narratives that hold up in administrative hearings.

**New skills required.** Track 1 (4 sessions). Track 6 micro-labs on policy search, drafting, and data extraction. Familiarization with the agency's mobile field tool integration (Phase 3).

### Example: Analyst (Policy / Budget / Performance)

**What stays the same.** You frame the question. You decide which data sources to trust. You write the recommendation. You stand in front of the council, board, or director and defend it.

**What gets faster.** Literature reviews. Extracting figures from agency reports. Drafting standard sections of recurring reports (year-over-year, comparable jurisdictions). Translating between formats — a council briefing into a public memo, a budget narrative into an FAQ.

**What becomes possible.** Higher-volume scenario analysis. More peer-jurisdiction comparisons. Deeper engagement with stakeholders during the analysis, not just at the presentation. Earlier draft cycles that improve final quality.

**New skills required.** Track 1 (4 sessions). Track 6 micro-labs on summarization, RAG-powered policy search, and pattern-spotting. Optional Track 4 sessions if the analyst writes code (uncommon but real).

### Example: Front-Line Customer Service (311, Permits, Library)

**What stays the same.** You are the human face of the agency. You make judgment calls about edge cases. You de-escalate. You listen to people who are frustrated, scared, or grieving. AI cannot do that and will not be asked to.

**What gets faster.** Drafting standard responses. Translating with non-English speakers (with you reviewing the translation). Looking up the right form, fee, or office. Triage and routing of incoming requests. Closing out cases with consistent documentation.

**What becomes possible.** More time on the genuinely complex cases. Faster handle times for the routine ones, which means shorter queues and better service. Career development time during quieter periods.

**New skills required.** Track 1 (4 sessions). Track 6 micro-labs on drafting, translation, and policy lookup. Familiarization with the agency's customer-facing chatbot if one is deployed.

### Example: IT / Engineering Staff

**What stays the same.** Architecture decisions. Security review. Production incident response. The judgment of when to deviate from standards. Mentoring junior staff.

**What gets faster.** First-draft code. Test scaffolding. Documentation. Code review for style issues. Refactoring known patterns. Ticket triage and incident note-taking.

**What becomes possible.** Higher-quality reviews of the code AI generates (because you have time you didn't have before). Better documentation. Faster time from intake to working prototype. More headroom to mentor non-IT staff in Track 1 facilitation.

**New skills required.** Tracks 1, 4, and one Track 5 (Champions) cohort spot.

## Manager talking points

Track 7 (Middle Managers) trains supervisors on these conversations. The talking points below are the cards they take into 1:1s.

### "Is the AI going to take my job?"

> "I don't think so, and the agency has committed in writing that it won't be the reason. The Director's letter from [date] says no AI-driven layoffs through [end date]. If something changes for Year 2, you'll know with at least 60 days' notice.
>
> What I do think is going to happen is your job will change. The repetitive parts — [name 2–3 specific tasks for this person] — will get faster. The part of your job that's about [judgment / relationships / quality] will become a bigger share of your week. That's the deal we're betting on.
>
> If you want to walk through the role evolution map for [role], I have it pulled up. Want to do that now or in our 1:1 next week?"

### "I tried it once and it was wrong / made stuff up"

> "Yeah — that happens, and it's a real issue. Part of the training is learning to spot when AI is confidently wrong, because it does that. The Track 6 lab on 'Is This AI Output Correct?' is specifically about this.
>
> What you should _not_ do is use AI for high-stakes work without a verification step. What you _should_ do is keep trying it on lower-stakes work and build a feel for where it's good and where it's not. We're not asking you to trust it. We're asking you to learn it."

### "I don't want to. I'm fine doing my job the way I've always done it."

> "That's a fair position, and nobody is going to discipline you for it. The agency has decided to invest in AI tools, and most staff will find at least one or two uses that genuinely save them time. If you don't, that's not a problem.
>
> What I would ask: try it once on something low-stakes. If it doesn't help, fine. If it does, you have one new tool. Either outcome is OK with me."

### "I'm worried about [bias / privacy / surveillance]."

> "Those are the right concerns to have, and they're exactly the concerns the Review Committee is paid to think about before any AI tool gets deployed. The Tier-3 use cases — the ones that affect rights, benefits, or safety — get the most scrutiny.
>
> If you have a specific concern about a specific tool, the [Equity Officer / Privacy Officer / Ombuds] will hear it confidentially. If you want to be on the Review Committee — including as a community advisor for Tier-3 reviews — let me know. We need that voice."

### "Why are we doing this when we have [budget / staffing / IT] problems already?"

> "Because in 12 months, the staff and budget problems will still be here, and AI will be more prevalent in our work whether we plan for it or not. The agencies that don't plan end up with shadow AI — staff using consumer tools with sensitive data because nothing approved exists. The agencies that do plan get to choose which uses are worth the investment.
>
> We're not deferring the other problems — we're making sure we're not adding 'unmanaged AI' to the list."

## Union and labor engagement

If your agency is unionized, coordinate with labor leadership before broad Phase 2 rollout. At minimum:

1. **Brief the union leadership** on the AI program scope, timeline, and the leadership commitment letter — _before_ the all-staff kickoff. They should not first hear about it the same time the membership does.
2. **Consider union representation in governance** where appropriate and consistent with labor rules. For many agencies, a labor seat, advisory role, or structured consultation step improves Tier-3 review and procurement legitimacy.
3. **Include AI-related labor protections in the next bargaining cycle.** Don't promise these in 1:1 conversations; ratify them in the contract. Topics to address: training time as paid work, displaced-staff retraining rights, opt-out for staff in roles where AI is being piloted, grievance process for AI-related concerns.

A unionized rollout that skips labor engagement risks grievance, impasse, or public opposition that can erase the trust the program needs.

## What to do if you cannot get the commitment letter

This is the bind some agency program leads find themselves in: the Director will sign vague language but won't commit to "no AI-driven layoffs." This is a serious signal. Three honest options:

1. **Reframe and renegotiate.** The Director may be worried about future budget constraints they can't predict. Offer a 12-month commitment with an explicit Year-2 review trigger. This often resolves it.
2. **Scope down.** If a full agency-wide commitment is not possible, get a department-level commitment for the specific units the program will train. Smaller, signable commitments are better than agency-wide vague ones.
3. **Defer broad Phase 2 training.** If neither works, pause the broad training program. Run only Phase 1 (governance), leadership briefings, and a small Champions cohort as a Year-1 program. Revisit staff training when leadership is ready to commit.

It is much cheaper to defer broad training than to run training that loses staff trust.

## Plain-English Guide to Job-Impact Terms

- **Augmentation vs. automation.** Augmentation = AI helps a human do the work faster or better, human still in the loop. Automation = AI does the work, human reviews the output (or doesn't). Most government work that AI touches is augmentation. Headlines about "automation" usually describe augmentation.
- **Displacement.** Used here narrowly — a staff member's specific workflow changing substantially because of AI. Different from layoff. Displacement triggers retraining and reassignment under the commitment letter; it does not trigger separation.
- **Retraining and reassignment.** Concrete: paid time to learn a new skill, paid time on a new assignment, no loss of pay or benefits during the transition.
- **Pulse survey.** A short (3-question) recurring measurement — not a satisfaction survey. Designed to catch behavior change, not opinion.
