The Champions Network is the agency's distributed change capacity. Champions are staff who volunteer (or are nominated) to be the first to try new tools, the people peers ask informally for help, and the early-warning system that catches what the AI program lead would otherwise miss. They are not trainers and not enforcers — they are the trusted neighbor in each team who has already tried the thing.

## Who champions are

The right champions are not the loudest enthusiasts in the agency. The pattern that works: respected mid-career staff who are credible because they do the work well, who are willing to try things, and whose peers will ask them privately for help. A few traits to look for:

- Treats AI as a tool, not a cause. Excited but not evangelistic.
- Already the person peers ask "how do you do X?" — even before AI.
- Comfortable saying "I tried it and it was wrong" out loud.
- Willing to commit ~2 hours/week to the role for at least 6 months.

The wrong champions: people who treat AI as identity (positive or negative), people who are status-seeking, and people whose peers don't already trust them. Manager nomination is usually a better signal than self-nomination, but the program accepts both.

## What champions do

The role is intentionally specific:

1. **Try first.** Champions get early access to new approved tools and are expected to use them on real work for ≥2 weeks before the broader rollout.
2. **Be findable.** Champions are listed in the agency directory with the AI champion tag. Peers can ask them anything, anytime.
3. **Surface intake candidates.** Champions are the conduit between informal team conversations and the [intake form](/phase-2-education/use-case-intake/). When a peer says "wouldn't it be great if X," champions either submit it or coach the peer to submit it.
4. **Report patterns.** At the monthly meetup, champions report what they're hearing — common questions, common failures, sentiment shifts. The AI program lead uses this as ground truth, often more reliable than survey data.
5. **Hold the standards.** Champions reinforce the AUP and the Tier framework informally. "Make sure that's not Tier-3 work before you put real names in" lands differently from a peer than from compliance staff.

What champions do NOT do: train, audit, evaluate other staff's work, or report on coworkers. The role is collaborative, not surveillance. Stating this clearly at onboarding is essential.

## The 4-hour onboarding workshop

Need onboarding support materials? Use the [Champions Onboarding Workshop deck source](/deck-sources/phase-2/track-5-champions/champions-onboarding-workshop.md) as a starting point. Paste or upload it into your preferred AI presentation tool, then localize, verify, and brand the generated deck before use.

Run at the start of each champion cohort. Structure:

- **Hour 1.** Why this network exists and what the role is (and isn't). Read of the leadership commitment letter, walk through the AUP and Tier matrix, discussion of the boundary between champion and HR/compliance roles.
- **Hour 2.** Tools deep dive. Champions get the same content as Track 1 sessions, accelerated, and access to whatever is in pilot at the time. Hands-on with at least 2 tools.
- **Hour 3.** Coaching peers. Workshop on the four resistance patterns from [Session 7.2](/phase-2-education/track-7-middle-managers/session-7-2/), adapted for peer-to-peer rather than manager-to-staff dynamics. Champions are not coaches in the formal sense, but they will encounter all four patterns and need a posture.
- **Hour 4.** Logistics, the monthly meetup format, the Slack/Teams channel norms, the intake form, and how to escalate. Each champion leaves with a concrete commitment: which team they cover, who their peer-champion partner is, what tool they will pilot first.

## Monthly meetups (90 minutes)

For a reusable meeting shell, use the [Monthly Champions Meetup deck source](/deck-sources/phase-2/track-5-champions/monthly-meetup-template.md) as a starting point. Paste or upload it into your preferred AI presentation tool, then localize, verify, and brand the generated deck before use.

The recurring rhythm is what makes the network durable. Suggested agenda:

- **15 min.** Round-the-room: one thing each champion heard or saw in the past month.
- **30 min.** Topic of the month — a new tool, a policy update, a case study from inside the agency or a peer agency.
- **30 min.** Working time — champions submit intake candidates from their teams together; the AI program lead is in the room and can triage live.
- **15 min.** Asks and offers. What does each champion need from the program? What is each champion offering this month?

Meetups should not become demos or training. The high-leverage portion is the round-the-room signal capture; protect it.

## Output discipline

The Champions Network is measured by what it produces, not by how many people sign up:

- ≥3 intake candidates per champion per quarter, sourced from peer conversations.
- ≥1 hour/week of informal peer help (self-reported, sanity-checked at monthly meetups).
- Sentiment ground truth quarterly: "what is your team actually saying about AI?" — written or recorded, sent to the program lead.

Champions who are not producing in any of these dimensions for two consecutive quarters get a quiet conversation about whether the role is still right for them. The exit is normalized — most champions serve 12–24 months, then rotate out. Burnout in this role is real and forced rotation is healthier than retention pressure.

## Recognition and incentives

Champion service is public on the agency intranet directory and recognized at year-end. Time spent is on-the-clock — managers are expected to honor the ~2 hours/week, and the AI program lead has explicit cover from leadership to push back on managers who don't.

Avoid: cash bonuses, promotion preference, or anything that turns the role into a status game. The champion network works because the people who self-select are intrinsically motivated; extrinsic rewards distort that.

## Evaluation

| Level | Metric                                                                      | Target                  |
| ----- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------- |
| 3     | Intake submissions per champion per quarter                                 | ≥3                      |
| 3     | Champion-attached departments outperform non-attached on Track 1 attendance | ≥1.5×                   |
| 3     | Peer help-request response time (self-reported via monthly meetup roll-up)  | <24 business hours      |
| 4     | Sentiment ground truth correlates with quarterly survey within ±10pt        | Yes (validates network) |
| 4     | Tier-2 pilots sourced from champion-surfaced ideas                          | ≥40% of pilots          |

## Common Track 5 failures

- **Letting the network become a help desk.** Champions are not Tier-1 support. If peers are routing all AI questions to champions, the program is missing self-service docs or the approved tool list. Fix the upstream gap.
- **Recruiting only enthusiasts.** A network entirely of evangelists doesn't represent the agency. Recruit credibly skeptical champions too — they catch real failure modes the enthusiasts miss.
- **No exit ramp.** Champions need a graceful way out at 12–24 months. Without it, the same five people carry the program for years and burn out.
- **Conflating champions with managers.** A line manager can be a champion only if the dual role is explicitly negotiated and the staff understand the distinction. In most cases, keep them separate — manager authority and champion peer-trust are easier to hold apart than to combine.
