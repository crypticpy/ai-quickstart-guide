The intake form is the single mechanism that connects training to action. Without it, training is a one-way push: people sit through sessions, learn things, then go back to work and nothing happens. With it, training produces a queue — and the queue is what your Review Committee actually approves into pilots, your Champions discuss in Track 5 meetings, and your Phase 6 starter projects come from.

Track 1 Session 1.4 ends with every attendee submitting a real intake form for a use case from their own work. That single act — taking five minutes to articulate "here is something I would actually use AI for" — is the strongest single predictor of whether a staff member will engage with the program for the next twelve months.

## What happens after you submit

1. **Email the exported file** to your AI Program Lead, or attach it to the agency's intake mailbox / SharePoint. (Each agency runs the inbox somewhere different — the form is the same.)
2. **Initial tier proposed** by the AI program lead based on your answers and the [Risk Classification Matrix](/phase-1-governance/risk-classification/).
3. **Review Committee confirms or escalates** at the next standing meeting. Tier-1 cases can be batch-approved on consent; Tier-2 require discussion; Tier-3 require a full impact assessment before approval.
4. **You will hear back** within 15 business days with a tier classification, a sponsor liaison from the committee, and a recommendation: pilot now, refine and resubmit, or hold for later.
5. **Tier is recorded** in the AI use case inventory along with the classifying meeting minutes.

If your idea is held for later, do not take it as a rejection. Most Tier-3 ideas hold for at least a quarter while the agency builds the controls (impact assessment, public notice, contestation pathway) needed to deploy them safely. Tier-1 ideas typically move within 4–6 weeks.

## Why these five questions

The form is short on purpose. Long intake forms produce two failure modes: staff don't fill them in, and the staff who do answer questions they don't yet have evidence for. Five questions catches the signal you need to triage:

| Question         | What it determines                                                       |
| ---------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Use case name    | Identifies the work product so it can be tracked across review cycles    |
| Sponsor identity | Names the person accountable for the idea moving forward                 |
| Problem          | Whether the idea is real work or speculation                             |
| Audience         | Whether the AI's output ever leaves the building                         |
| Decision impact  | Whether AI is advisory or load-bearing                                   |
| Data sensitivity | Whether the data alone moves the use case to a higher tier               |

The full risk classification (the [Risk Tier Picker](/phase-1-governance/risk-classification/)) is a five-dimension instrument. The intake form covers the three dimensions that staff can answer reliably from their own knowledge: audience, decision impact, and data. The other two dimensions (rights/benefits/safety, reversibility) require committee review against legal and policy context, so the form does not ask about them — it lets the committee make that call.

## What sponsors should expect

If your use case is **Tier 1**, expect:

- A 1–2 sentence reply from the AI program lead noting the use case is on the next consent agenda.
- Approval at the next bi-weekly committee meeting.
- A short email back: "Approved for the Approved AI Tools List, Tier-1 use. Logged in inventory."
- You can start using the tool within ~2 weeks.

If your use case is **Tier 2**, expect:

- A scheduled 15–30 minute discussion with you (the sponsor) at the next committee meeting.
- A request for the workflow documented in slightly more detail: who uses it, how often, what triggers it, what the output looks like.
- A pilot approval (4–8 weeks) before broad deployment, often with success metrics defined.
- A vendor-side check if the use case requires a procurement addendum.

If your use case is **Tier 3**, expect:

- A scheduled deep-dive with the committee plus legal and the equity officer, typically 60–90 minutes.
- A request for an impact assessment — the committee can help you produce one, but it takes time.
- Public-notice and contestation requirements drafted before any deployment.
- The fastest realistic path from idea to limited pilot is roughly one quarter.

None of these timelines are punishments. They are the time the committee needs to do the review properly. Slower review on Tier 3 prevents the kind of failure that kills the entire program.

## Common intake mistakes (and how to fix them)

| Mistake                                                         | Fix                                                                                            |
| --------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| "I want to use AI for [department]" — too broad                 | Pick one workflow. The narrower, the easier to triage.                                         |
| "AI will help us be more efficient" — no measurable outcome     | What does success look like? "Cut intake summary time from 30 min to 5 min" is a real outcome. |
| "We need an AI" — solution looking for a problem                | Start with the problem. "We can't keep up with constituent emails" leads to a use case.         |
| Sponsor is a vendor or external consultant                      | Sponsor must be agency staff with operational accountability for the workflow.                  |
| Data sensitivity left blank ("not sure")                        | If you don't know, talk to your records officer or CISO before submitting. The committee will ask. |

## Privacy and data handling

The intake form runs entirely in your browser. Specifically:

- Your answers are saved to your browser's localStorage so you can come back later and finish.
- Nothing is sent to a server when you fill in fields. The internet tab can be closed; the form still works.
- The "Export as Markdown" button generates the file from your browser memory. The download happens on your machine.
- "Print / save as PDF" uses your browser's standard print dialog.
- "Reset" clears your localStorage entry.

For agencies that need a centralized queue, the typical flow is: staff fills in the form here, exports to markdown, emails the file to the AI program lead's mailbox, lead drops the markdown into a shared SharePoint or git repo. Some agencies have built a thin server-side intake collector; that is appropriate for Phase 5 (Platform), not for Phase 2 quickstart.
