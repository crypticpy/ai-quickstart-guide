Most public-sector AI training programs work for the first six months and then die quietly. Not in a single moment — by attrition. The launch energy fades, the Director moves on to the next initiative, the program lead is reassigned, the early adopters are using AI productively while everyone else has forgotten the training they sat through. By Month 9 the intake pipeline is empty and the Year 2 budget conversation starts with "we tried that."

This playbook is the set of low-cost, high-leverage mechanisms that prevent that outcome. It runs Months 7–12 of Year 1 and is the bridge to Year 2.

## The five mechanisms

| #   | Mechanism                  | Frequency      | Owner                     | Cost                     |
| --- | -------------------------- | -------------- | ------------------------- | ------------------------ |
| 1   | Monthly Lunch & Learns     | Monthly, Mo 7+ | Champions (Track 5)       | 20 min staff time / dept |
| 2   | Peer Storytelling Program  | Monthly        | Comms + Champions         | 1 story / month          |
| 3   | Pioneer Recognition        | Monthly        | Director / CIO            | 5 min in all-hands       |
| 4   | Quarterly Engagement Pulse | Quarterly      | AI program lead           | 3-question survey        |
| 5   | Month 13 Handoff Plan      | Once, Mo 12    | AI program lead + sponsor | 1 working session        |

Each mechanism is small. Skipping any one of them rarely kills the program; skipping several of them raises the risk that adoption fades after launch.

## How this page scales for your agency

| Path | Minimum useful version | Add when capacity exists |
| ---- | ---------------------- | ------------------------ |
| Small / no dedicated IT | One monthly demo in an existing meeting, one named owner, one quarterly pulse | Peer story every other month |
| Medium | Lunch & Learns, Champions recognition, quarterly pulse, Month 13 handoff memo | Department-level intake review and refresher labs |
| Large / federated | Formal Champions network, recognition cadence, engagement reporting by division | Year-2 budget line and rotating department playbooks |

## 1. Monthly Lunch & Learns

A 20-minute Lunch & Learn on AI, embedded inside an _existing_ department staff meeting — not a new meeting. Led by a Champion from that department, not by the AI program lead. Focused on one real use case the staff in the room actually face.

**Why this works.** New meetings on a new initiative get the lowest priority on a busy calendar. A 20-minute slot inside a meeting people already attend gets much higher attendance.

**Format.**

- **5 min** — A peer talks about a specific AI workflow they're using ("Here's how I've been using the summarizer for case file intake")
- **5 min** — Live demo of the workflow, with realistic-but-public sample data
- **5 min** — One audience member tries it themselves, on the spot
- **5 min** — Open Q&A, plus "what would you want to see next month?"

**Cadence per department.** One Lunch & Learn per month, every month from Month 7 forward. Skipping a month is fine; skipping two in a row is a trigger to check in with the department's Champion.

**What to avoid.**

- The AI program lead facilitating. This is Reinforcement work — peer voice matters more than expert voice.
- Slides. The format is a conversation, not a presentation.
- Tools the audience does not have access to. Demonstrate only what staff can use.

## 2. Peer storytelling program

One real adoption story per month, told by the staff member who lived it, distributed in whatever channel staff already read.

**The story format.**

> _Sarah Chen, AP Specialist, Finance Department_
>
> _"I was spending about three hours a week reconciling vendor invoices to the contract terms — pulling up the contract, finding the line item, comparing it against what came in on the invoice. I started using the policy-search tool we got in Phase 3 to pull up the relevant contract clause, and the document AI to extract structured data from the invoice. Now I do the reconciliation in about 20 minutes a week. The time I got back, I'm using to follow up on aging vendor disputes that had been sitting in the queue for months. We cleared 14 of them last quarter."_

**Why this works.** Same-role stories hit harder than any trainer-delivered content. Sarah Chen in AP convinces her peers in AP. The CIO convinces nobody.

**Production cost.** A 30-minute interview with the staff member, written up by Comms or by a Champion in 90 minutes, edited and approved in 30 minutes. About 2 hours of staff time per story.

**Distribution.** Where staff already are: agency newsletter, Teams channel, intranet front page, monthly all-hands. Not a new email list nobody opens.

**What to avoid.**

- Stories that sound rehearsed or sanitized. If the staff member says "It works most of the time but it gets confused on multi-page invoices," keep that line.
- Manager-told stories about their team. "My team uses AI now" lands flat. The staff member tells the story.
- Quarterly instead of monthly. Cadence builds the expectation; once-in-a-while feels like marketing.

## 3. Pioneer recognition

Monthly all-hands shoutout, by the Director or CIO personally, of staff who shipped something with AI in the last month.

**What counts as a "pioneer."**

- Staff who submitted a use case that moved through Tier 2 review.
- Staff who completed a Domain Lab series and applied it on actual work.
- Staff who became Champions and led a Lunch & Learn.
- Staff who reported a problem with an AI tool that led to a change in the agency's deployment.

**Format (60 seconds, in the standing all-hands meeting).**

> "Last month, [Name] in [Department] shipped [specific thing]. That kind of work — trying something, telling us what worked and what didn't, helping the rest of us learn — is what makes this program function. Thank you, [Name]."

**Why this works.** Status, belonging, permission to try things. Public recognition by the senior-most person in the room is one of the highest-leverage and lowest-cost interventions in change management. The CIO's 60 seconds is more valuable than the program lead's 60 minutes.

**What to avoid.**

- Recognition by the AI program lead instead of the executive sponsor. Same content, much less impact.
- Recognition that becomes a script. If the same three names get called every month, staff stop listening.
- "Failure recognition" alongside it. Mixing "Sarah shipped a great workflow" with "we had a bad outcome on this project, we're learning from it" undercuts both messages. Keep them separate.

## 4. Quarterly engagement pulse

A three-question survey, run at Months 3, 6, 9, and 12. Anonymous. Three questions, scored 1–5. Takes staff under two minutes to complete.

**The three questions.**

1. _Do you feel supported in learning AI in your role?_
2. _Have you used an AI tool in your work in the last 30 days?_ (Yes / No, no scale)
3. _Do you understand how AI is going to affect your role over the next 12 months?_

**Threshold.** If any of the scaled questions averages **below 3.5 out of 5.0** at any quarterly check, that triggers a pause-and-review.

**The pause-and-review protocol.**

- The AI program lead and the executive sponsor meet within 2 weeks of the pulse.
- They review the open-ended responses (always include one optional free-text box: _What would help most?_).
- Technical phases that depend on cultural readiness — new tool rollouts, new training pushes — pause for 30–60 days.
- The agency runs a structured listening tour to identify the cause: Awareness gap? Resistance pattern? Bad early experience with a specific tool? Manager not engaging?
- A targeted intervention is launched, and the next pulse measures whether it worked.

**Why a small survey beats a big one.** Three questions get answered. Twelve questions get half-answered. The point is to detect a problem early, not to produce a comprehensive picture. The qualitative listening tour produces the picture; the pulse is the alarm.

**What to avoid.**

- Running the pulse and ignoring the results. This is the single most damaging move — staff learn the survey is for show, and your engagement goes down regardless of what the program does.
- Adding "satisfaction" questions ("How satisfied are you with the AI program?"). Satisfaction is downstream of behavior change. Measure the behavior; satisfaction will follow if the behavior is real.
- Running the pulse without committing to the response protocol in advance. If you do not have agreement that low scores trigger a pause, you will be talked out of pausing when scores are low.

## 5. Month 13 handoff plan

Before Year 1 ends, document the answer to four questions:

1. **Who owns ongoing AI training in Year 2?** Named individual, with budget and time allocated. Not "the AI program" or "IT generally."
2. **What is the Year 2 budget?** Concrete dollar figure. Includes training facilitation, Champion stipends or recognition, tool subscriptions, and a contingency for response to engagement pulses.
3. **Which tracks continue, which archive, which evolve?** Track 1 Foundations almost always continues for new hires. Tracks 2 and 3 typically run on a refresh cycle (annual). Track 4 evolves as the dev stack matures. Track 5 (Champions) and Track 6 (Domain Labs) are the long-term reinforcement engines.
4. **How do new hires onboard to the AI culture?** Embedded in agency new-hire onboarding within their first 30 days. Tracks 1.1–1.3 plus a current intake form walkthrough.

**Why this matters.** Without a handoff plan, the program reverts to "the AI thing the previous program lead ran." Even if everyone agrees it should continue, with no named owner and no budget line, it doesn't. Fund the handoff.

**Format.** A two-page memo, signed by the executive sponsor and the program lead, distributed to the senior leadership team and the Review Committee. Reviewed at each quarterly all-hands in Year 2 to confirm the plan is on track.

Six Lunch & Learns per department, six peer stories, six recognition moments, two pulses, one budget conversation, one handoff plan. That's the entire Reinforcement-stage workload.

## Remediation and support paths

Not everyone succeeds on the first try. Silence about failure breeds shame and attrition. The sustainability plan includes explicit support for people who struggle:

- **Track 1 (Foundations).** If quiz scores are below 60%, offer a 1-on-1 30-minute office hour with the facilitator to review concepts. No penalty, no public reporting. The goal is understanding, not gatekeeping.
- **Track 4 (Developers).** If a lab isn't completed, pair the developer with a buddy who finished. Provide an extra week before the next lab. Capstone deadline is flexible — better to ship late than never.
- **Track 7 (Managers).** If a manager struggles with coaching conversations, offer a private practice session with the change management lead. Provide scripted talking points they can read verbatim until they're comfortable.
- **General principle.** Never publicly identify who is behind. Offer help proactively ("Would an extra session be helpful?") rather than reactively ("You failed the quiz"). Frame it as "additional support," not "remediation."

## Year 2 — what evolves

By Month 13, the program shifts from launch to operations. The pattern most agencies converge on:

- **Annual refresh of Tracks 2 (Leadership) and 3 (Governance).** Re-orient new directors and counsel; update for new state and federal regulation.
- **Continuous Tracks 1 (Foundations) and 7 (Managers)** for new hires and newly-promoted supervisors. Run quarterly cohorts.
- **Domain Labs (Track 6)** become a standing program. New labs added when the intake pipeline shows recurring patterns.
- **Champions Network (Track 5)** continues monthly. Champions rotate annually so leadership doesn't ossify.
- **Tracks 4 (Developers)** evolves into a more focused community-of-practice as the platform (Phase 5) matures. Less curriculum, more code review and ADR discussion.

The Year 2 budget is often lower than the Year 1 launch budget because most curriculum content is already produced. Still plan for facilitation time, Champion support, recognition, tool access, and response to pulse findings.

## Common failures and recovery

| Failure                                                   | Recovery                                                                                                                                                                     |
| --------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Lunch & Learns dwindle to one or two engaged departments  | Retire dormant ones gracefully. Concentrate on departments with active intake submissions; rebuild outward.                                                                  |
| Peer stories all sound the same (early adopters only)     | Recruit stories from staff who almost gave up and came back. The "I tried it, it was bad, then I tried again" story builds more trust than the success story.                |
| Pulse drops at Month 9 and the program does not pause     | Acknowledge the miss publicly. Run the pause-and-review one quarter late. Trust takes longer to rebuild, but the alternative — never pausing — destroys the pulse as a tool. |
| Director leaves; new Director has not been briefed        | First conversation is not "we need budget." First conversation is a 30-minute walkthrough of what's working and what's at risk. Budget follows credibility, not the reverse. |
| Champion network ossifies — same six people for two years | Term-limit champions to 12–18 months with explicit "graduation" recognition. Recruit replacements from recent intake submitters.                                             |

## Plain-English Guide to Sustainability Terms

- **Reinforcement** — The fifth ADKAR stage. Mechanisms that keep a behavior in place after it has been adopted. Distinct from "more training."
- **Lunch & Learn** — A short (15–25 minute) skill-sharing session embedded in an existing meeting. Distinct from a workshop.
- **Engagement pulse** — A short, recurring survey designed to catch behavior change early. Distinct from a satisfaction survey.
- **Pause-and-review** — A pre-committed protocol to slow technical work when cultural readiness drops. Distinct from "we decided to take a break."
- **Handoff plan** — The named owner, budget, and scope for Year 2. Distinct from "the program continues" (which is not a plan).
