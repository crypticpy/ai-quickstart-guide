Prosci reports that human factors are a major driver of failed AI and digital-change efforts. Your tooling can be flawless and your governance airtight; if you skip the change-management work, the rollout will still stall. This playbook is the operational guide for facilitators, managers, and the AI program lead through the five ADKAR stages.

## What ADKAR is, in one paragraph

ADKAR (Awareness, Desire, Knowledge, Ability, Reinforcement) is a sequenced model: people cannot want a change they don't understand, cannot learn what they don't want, cannot apply what they haven't learned, and cannot sustain what isn't reinforced. Skipping a stage doesn't speed things up — it produces the symptom of that stage's neglect months later.

## Stage map

| Stage             | What it produces                                | Phase 2 vehicle                                        | Failure symptom                                                         |
| ----------------- | ----------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------ | ----------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Awareness**     | "I know AI is coming and roughly what it means" | Track 1 Sessions 1.1–1.2; leadership commitment letter | Staff hear about AI from social media before they hear from leadership  |
| **Desire**        | "I want to use this — or at least try"          | Track 1 Sessions 1.3–1.4; Track 2; manager coaching    | Quiet refusal: people attend training but don't engage with the tools   |
| **Knowledge**     | "I know how to use it for my actual work"       | Tracks 3, 4, 6, 7 + Domain Labs                        | Staff use AI but produce confidently wrong outputs                      |
| **Ability**       | "I can use it well, on my own, under deadline"  | Hands-on labs, Champions network, in-flow coaching     | Staff revert to old workflows under stress; AI gets used only for demos |
| **Reinforcement** | "I keep using it because the system rewards it" | Sustainability playbook, recognition, embedded reviews | Adoption fades after Month 6; intake pipeline goes silent               |

## Awareness — Months 0–2

**Goal.** Every staff member can answer three questions in plain language before any training begins:

1. What is the agency planning to do with AI?
2. Why are we doing it?
3. What does this mean for me, specifically?

If staff cannot answer #3, you do not have Awareness — you have rumor.

### What to ship

- **Leadership commitment letter** (see [Job-Impact Messaging](/phase-2-education/job-impact-messaging/)). Signed by Director / CIO. One page. States the no-AI-driven-layoffs commitment for the 12-month program.
- **All-staff kickoff** (45 min, virtual or in-person). Director presents the letter, the program lead walks through the 12-month roadmap, Q&A is unfiltered. Record it for staff who can't attend live.
- **FAQ document** maintained in a wiki page or shared site. Updated with every question the program lead is asked more than twice.

### What goes wrong

- **The CIO sends an email instead of holding a kickoff.** Staff read between the lines and assume the worst.
- **Comms department writes the language.** It comes out as marketing copy — "exciting AI journey" — and undermines credibility.
- **The commitment letter is missing or hedged.** Anything weaker than "no AI-driven layoffs during the 12-month program" reads as "layoffs are coming, we just won't admit it."

> **Recovery.** If you've already lost Awareness — staff are anxious, rumors are circulating — pause and run a structured listening tour. Five 30-minute small-group sessions across departments, no slides, just questions. Then issue a revised commitment letter that addresses the concerns by name. Don't try to fix Awareness with more emails.

## Desire — Months 1–3

**Goal.** A meaningful share of staff actively wants to engage with AI tools — not just tolerate them, but submit ideas, attend optional sessions, ask questions in 1:1s. Set the numeric target locally; 30% is a useful planning benchmark, not a universal requirement.

Desire is built socially, not informationally. It comes from peers, not from training departments. The interventions that work are the ones that produce visible peers using AI well.

### What to ship

- **Track 1 Sessions 1.3 and 1.4** — "Your Job + AI" and the intake form session. The shift from passive learning to "here is something I personally would use" is the Desire moment.
- **First Champions cohort identified and named.** People want to be on a list. Being named a "Champion" produces engagement before any training has happened.
- **Manager coaching cards** (from Track 7) so supervisors can have one-on-one conversations with staff who are skeptical, anxious, overenthusiastic, or quietly disengaged.

### Resistance patterns and how to handle them

| Pattern                | Looks like                                                                    | Don't do             | Do                                                                                           |
| ---------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **The skeptic**        | "I've seen this before — it's hype that goes away in two years"               | Argue or oversell    | Acknowledge their experience; ask what would change their mind; let small wins speak         |
| **The anxious**        | Quiet, asks "is my job safe" in side conversations, doesn't speak in sessions | Reassure generically | Have a private conversation; share the role evolution map for their specific role            |
| **The overenthusiast** | Wants to use AI for everything immediately, including Tier-3 work             | Squash enthusiasm    | Channel: ask them to submit three intake forms, including one risky one for committee review |
| **The passive**        | Attends sessions, nods, never tries anything                                  | Mandate compliance   | Pair with a Champion for one specific task; small commitment, peer pressure                  |

### What goes wrong

- **Mandatory training without optional follow-up.** If the only AI exposure staff have is mandatory, they associate AI with compliance, not opportunity.
- **The first wave of Champions is all from IT.** Champions need to be peers across functions — accounts payable, library staff, code enforcement. IT champions don't move non-IT staff.
- **Leadership uses Desire-stage language ("we're excited about AI") in Awareness contexts.** Staff still anxious about layoffs hear this as tone-deaf.

> **Recovery.** If Desire is low at end of Month 2 — Track 1 attendance below 60%, intake submissions in single digits — slow down. Don't push more training. Run a Champions-led peer tour: 4–5 short ad-hoc demos in regular team meetings, hosted by a peer not a trainer. Build social proof before pushing more content.

## Knowledge — Months 2–6

**Goal.** Staff know how to use AI for their actual work — not just generally, but with the prompts, the tools, and the limits relevant to their role.

This is the stage most curricula optimize for. It is also the stage where the gap between "I attended a training" and "I changed how I work" widens fastest.

### What to ship

- **Track 3** for governance / legal / compliance: each session produces a real artifact (risk matrix, AUP, charter, procurement addendum, compliance map).
- **Track 6 Domain Labs**: 45-minute sessions embedded in existing staff meetings, focused on one workflow at a time (summarization, drafting, extraction, translation, search, pattern-spotting, evaluation, prompting).
- **Track 7 Sessions 7.1 and 7.2** for managers — coaching frameworks and resistance handling.
- **Track 1 Session 1.4 → intake form** as the connective tissue. Knowledge that doesn't produce a submitted use case has not transferred.

### What goes wrong

- **Generic training instead of role-specific.** "Here is how to use ChatGPT" is not Knowledge. "Here is how an inspector uses AI to draft a violation notice for code §13-4-205" is Knowledge.
- **Training that uses the right tools but forbidden data.** Demonstrate AI workflows with realistic public data, not staff PII or actual case files. Otherwise you are training shadow-AI behavior.
- **Long-form training instead of short and frequent.** A 4-hour workshop produces less behavior change than four 45-minute embedded sessions over four weeks.

> **Recovery.** If Knowledge is low (low quiz scores, vague intake submissions, AI tools used for trivial tasks only), the fix is not more lecture. Run a Domain Lab series tied to actual recurring work. Measure pre/post completion time on a real task — not a quiz.

## Ability — Months 3–9

**Goal.** Staff can use AI tools effectively under real conditions — at full caseload, under deadline, with the messy data and partial information of actual work, not curated training scenarios.

This is the longest stage and the one most agencies underfund. Ability is built through deliberate practice, in-flow coaching, and explicit permission to fail.

### What to ship

- **Champions Network monthly meetups** (Track 5) — peer problem-solving on real cases.
- **Pair-programming or pair-using model**: staff trying a new AI workflow do their first three uses with a Champion shadowing.
- **Office hours** — weekly drop-in (30 min) hosted by AI program lead or Champions. Low-stakes "I tried this and got stuck" conversations.
- **Explicit failure protocol**: if an AI output produces a wrong outcome, the response is a recorded post-mortem and a process update — never staff discipline when policy was followed. Leadership should make that visible through behavior, not just written policy.

### What goes wrong

- **No deliberate practice.** Staff are taught at, never required to do guided practice on real work.
- **Punishing early failure.** A single highly-publicized AI mistake can produce a year of risk aversion. Leadership should visibly defend staff who followed policy and got a bad output.
- **No buddy system.** Solo learners under deadline pressure revert to the old workflow. Pair them.

> **Recovery.** If Ability is low, the diagnostic is "what does someone do at 4:55 PM on a Friday with three things on their desk?" If the answer is "the old way," your Ability rollout has failed. Increase pair-using density and reduce the number of new tools — fewer tools, more practice with each.

## Reinforcement — Months 6–12+

**Goal.** AI-augmented work is the default, not the exception. New hires onboard into AI-using practices. Bad AI workflows get fixed, not abandoned. Staff who started early are visibly recognized and progressing.

This is the stage where most public-sector training programs fail invisibly — Reinforcement requires steady management attention long after the launch energy is gone.

### What to ship

See the [Sustainability Playbook](/phase-2-education/sustainability/) for the full set:

- Monthly Lunch & Learns embedded in existing staff meetings (not new meetings).
- Peer storytelling program — one real adoption story per month, told by the peer, not the trainer.
- Pioneer recognition — monthly all-hands shoutout. Status, belonging, permission.
- Quarterly engagement pulse (Months 3, 6, 9, 12). Three questions, scored 1–5, scores below 3.5 trigger a pause and review.
- Month 13 handoff plan: who owns ongoing training in Year 2, what budget is allocated, how new hires onboard.

### What goes wrong

- **Reinforcement is treated as "Comms work" instead of leadership work.** Recognition by the AI program lead has 1/10 the impact of recognition by the Director.
- **Engagement pulses are run but ignored.** If the Month 6 pulse drops to 3.0 and you keep the Phase 3/4 schedule, you are training staff that the survey is for show.
- **No succession plan.** The AI program lead leaves, the program quietly dies, and the next hire is told "we tried that, it didn't work."

> **Recovery.** Reinforcement failures rarely have a single recovery move. They are rebuilt in the same way they should have been built — slowly, by giving the program a senior leader who shows up monthly and visibly champions the work, and by making sustainability a named agenda item in quarterly reviews.

## Mapping ADKAR to Kirkpatrick

The two models complement each other: ADKAR describes what's happening in the staff member; Kirkpatrick describes what's happening in your data.

| ADKAR stage   | Kirkpatrick level  | What you measure                                                          |
| ------------- | ------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Awareness     | Level 1 (Reaction) | Did staff feel the kickoff was clear and credible?                        |
| Desire        | Level 1 + opt-in   | Are staff voluntarily engaging (intake submissions, optional attendance)? |
| Knowledge     | Level 2 (Learning) | Quiz scores, artifact quality, intake form quality                        |
| Ability       | Level 3 (Behavior) | Are staff using AI for the demonstrated workflows in actual work?         |
| Reinforcement | Level 4 (Results)  | Time-savings, intake throughput, retention, year-over-year trend          |

If you are only measuring Level 1 (smile sheets), you are measuring whether people enjoyed the session. That tells you nothing about whether the program is working.

## When to pause the rollout

Three triggers should prompt a review and, where practical, pause technical phases:

1. **Quarterly engagement pulse drops below 3.5/5.0** on any of the three questions ("Do you feel supported in learning AI?" / "Have you used an AI tool in your work this month?" / "Do you understand how AI will affect your role?").
2. **A high-visibility AI failure** — wrong public-facing output, biased automated decision, leaked staff data — that has not yet been publicly addressed.
3. **A senior champion leaves** — the Director, CIO, or program lead departs without a named successor to the program.

Pausing means: stop adding new tools, stop running new training, hold a short stabilization sprint, and resume when the trigger is resolved or a documented mitigation is in place. Pushing through without review increases the risk of failure.

## Plain-English Guide to Change Terms

- **ADKAR** — A five-stage change model from Prosci (a change-management consultancy). Stands for the five stages a person goes through to adopt a change: Awareness, Desire, Knowledge, Ability, Reinforcement.
- **Kirkpatrick Model** — A four-level training evaluation framework (Reaction, Learning, Behavior, Results) widely used in government training programs. Predates AI by sixty years; still works.
- **Engagement pulse** — A short (3-question) survey run quarterly to measure whether the change is sticking. Different from a satisfaction survey: it asks about behavior, not feelings.
- **Resistance pattern** — A predictable shape of pushback (skeptic, anxious, overenthusiast, passive). Naming the pattern lets managers respond appropriately instead of escalating.
- **Champion** — A self-selected or nominated peer advocate within a department. Not a trainer; a credible co-worker who helps others use the tools.
