> **A note on sourcing.** These three case studies are composites — built from interviews and published reports across multiple agencies, then anonymized. The numbers, timelines, and decision points are realistic for agencies of the stated size; the specific agency names are not real. We chose the composite format because most agencies that successfully run a 12-month adoption can't or won't publish the full internal narrative, and a half-told story is less useful than a fully-told composite.

## Why three sizes

The PRD defines three agency-size variants — small (1–4 IT staff, ~18 months), medium (5–15 IT staff, ~12 months), and large (15+ IT staff, ~9 months) — because the same six phases play out very differently depending on what you start with. A small agency's hardest phase is usually Governance (no precedent, no in-house legal, executive sponsor wears five hats); a medium agency's hardest phase is Infrastructure (no platform team, ad-hoc cloud, change-management drag); a large agency's hardest phase is the Modular Platform (turf, existing systems, inner-source culture).

Each case study below walks through the same six phases, but with the time, money, and friction realistic for that agency size. Read whichever is closest to your situation first; skim the other two for the failure modes you might inherit if your agency grows or shrinks during the engagement.

## What they have in common

Reading all three side-by-side surfaces a few things every agency hit, regardless of size:

1. **The first month is mostly listening, not building.** All three started with a readiness assessment, an executive briefing, and a use-case intake pipeline before touching any tools. The agencies that tried to invert this order — pilot first, governance later — all had to redo the pilot once governance caught up.
2. **The hardest hire is not the AI engineer.** It's the mid-career person who can run an AI Review Committee meeting where legal, equity, and the requesting department are in the same room. All three agencies underestimated this.
3. **The "AI tool" is rarely the bottleneck.** Procurement was the bottleneck in two of three; data quality was the bottleneck in the third. Time spent on prompt engineering was a rounding error in all three.
4. **Off-ramps are real, not euphemism.** The small county genuinely paused at Phase 2 and shipped no production AI in Year 1 — and walked away with an adopted policy, a compliant tool list, and trained staff. That's a complete deliverable, not a failure.
5. **ROI in Year 1 was lower than projected; Year 2 caught up.** All three saw payback periods come in 30–60% slower than their initial estimates, primarily because adoption ramped slower than expected. The agencies that re-ran the [ROI Calculator](/resources/roi-calculator/) at month 6 with actuals had a much easier time defending Year 2 budget.

## How to use these

- **As a sponsor:** Read the case study closest to your size end-to-end. Use the year-end results section as the anchor for what's realistic for _your_ board / council to expect at the 12-month mark.
- **As a project lead:** Skim all three for the "what we'd do differently" sections. The patterns repeat across sizes.
- **As a procurement officer:** Each case study calls out the specific procurement vehicle that worked (state contract, NASPO, sole-source justification). These are jurisdiction-specific but the patterns transfer.
