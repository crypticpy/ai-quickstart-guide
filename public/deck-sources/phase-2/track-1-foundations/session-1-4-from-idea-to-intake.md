# Slide 1: From Idea To Intake

Main point: Staff ideas become useful AI projects when they are specific enough to review.

Bullets:

- Good ideas start from real workflows
- Intake makes ideas visible
- Review protects staff and the public
- Not every idea becomes a project

Speaker notes:

Set expectations. Intake is not a suggestion box where every idea is promised a build. It is the way the agency learns what workflows matter and which ones are feasible.

# Slide 2: Why Intake Matters

Main point: Intake gives the agency a fair, reviewable path for AI ideas.

Bullets:

- Avoids side-channel pilots
- Captures data and risk early
- Helps prioritize limited capacity
- Creates a record of decisions

Speaker notes:

Explain that intake is a service to staff, not a barrier. It helps the program lead route ideas to the right review level.

# Slide 3: What Makes A Strong Submission

Main point: Strong submissions describe the current work, not just the desired tool.

Bullets:

- Current workflow
- Pain point or bottleneck
- Data involved
- People affected
- Human review plan

Speaker notes:

Discourage "we need a chatbot" as a complete idea. A strong submission says what problem exists today and what would count as improvement. Success measures can be captured in the form or worksheet.

# Slide 4: Start With The Workflow

Main point: The workflow tells reviewers whether AI is a useful fit.

Bullets:

- Who does the work?
- How often does it happen?
- What takes the most time?
- Where do errors or delays occur?

Speaker notes:

Ask staff to describe the process as it works today. The best AI ideas often come from repetitive, document-heavy, or search-heavy steps.

Image guidance:

# Slide 5: Name The Data

Main point: The data determines the risk and review path.

Bullets:

- Public
- Internal
- Confidential
- Regulated or sensitive
- Unknown or mixed

Speaker notes:

Staff do not need to classify perfectly. They need to disclose what they know and ask when uncertain. Unknown data should be treated cautiously.

Image guidance:

Data classification ladder with "ask if unsure."

Evidence and review notes:

Align with local data classification.

# Slide 6: Name The Human Review Point

Main point: A responsible AI idea says who checks the output before it matters.

Bullets:

- Who reviews the output?
- What do they check?
- What happens if AI is wrong?
- Can the person override it?

Speaker notes:

This is the difference between a useful assistant and an unsafe automation. Human review should be concrete, not just "staff will check it."

Image guidance:

AI output passing through a human review checkpoint before final action.

No external claims.

# Slide 7: Live Demo - Risk Tier Picker

Main point: The risk-tier picker helps staff see why data, audience, and decision impact matter.

Bullets:

- Open the local risk-tier picker
- Use one realistic example
- Change one field at a time
- Watch the provisional tier change

Speaker notes:

Show the matrix or interactive picker from the risk classification page. Emphasize the rule of thumb: if a real person can lose a benefit, job, custody, housing, freedom, or money because of the AI output, the use is high risk.

# Slide 9: Common Intake Mistakes

Main point: Weak submissions are usually too broad, too tool-centered, or missing risk details.

Bullets:

- Too broad
- No measurable outcome
- Solution looking for a problem
- Vendor as sponsor
- Data sensitivity left blank

Speaker notes:

Reading these examples aloud is kinder than letting staff discover them in a rejection email. Keep the tone coaching-oriented.

# Slide 11: Activity - Draft Your Intake

Main point: Each attendee should leave with a draft idea or a clear reason they do not have one yet.

Bullets:

- Pick one workflow
- Fill the required fields
- Mark unknowns honestly
- Pair-review for clarity
- Submit or save for manager review

Speaker notes:

Give attendees time to work. The output can be a submitted form, a draft to finish later, or "we audited and found no good candidate."

Image guidance:

Activity instruction slide.

Evidence and review notes:

Confirm whether live submission is appropriate for the cohort.

# Slide 12: What Happens Next And Final Quiz

Main point: Staff should know where ideas go after submission.

Bullets:

- Intake received by [owner]
- Provisional risk tier assigned
- Clarifying questions if needed
- Review Committee considers candidates
- Final quiz covers Sessions 1.1-1.4

Speaker notes:

Close the loop. If staff submit ideas and never hear back, intake loses credibility quickly. Name the response target as a local placeholder, not a universal promise. Reserve time for the Track 1 final quiz after the activity.

Image guidance:

Status flow: received, needs clarification, under review, approved for discovery, deferred, declined.

Replace with actual status names, owner, response target, communication channel, and quiz logistics.
