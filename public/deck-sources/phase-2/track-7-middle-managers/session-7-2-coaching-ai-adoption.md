# Slide 1: Coaching AI Adoption

Main point: Managers help staff move from concern or curiosity into safe practice.

Bullets:

- Adoption is human before it is technical
- Resistance patterns are predictable
- Coaching beats arguing
- Psychological safety matters

Speaker notes:

Set the frame: managers are not expected to sell AI. They are expected to make it safe for staff to ask, try, report failures, and follow policy.

# Slide 2: What Psychological Safety Means Here

Main point: Staff need to be able to name mistakes, concerns, and confusion without fear of punishment when they followed policy.

Bullets:

- Ask questions early
- Report bad outputs
- Admit uncertainty
- Pause risky use
- Escalate concerns

Speaker notes:

This is not permission to ignore policy. It is the condition that lets the agency learn from mistakes before they become incidents.

Image guidance:

Safe-to-raise-hand visual, professional and restrained.

Evidence and review notes:

Align with HR and reporting policy.

# Slide 3: Pattern 1 - Skeptic

Main point: Skepticism can improve the program when managers treat it as useful evidence.

Bullets:

- "It made things up"
- "This is vendor hype"
- "It will create more work"
- Response: validate, test, and gather examples

Speaker notes:

Do not try to win skeptics with slogans. Ask for specific examples and invite them into low-risk evaluation.

Image guidance:

Scenario card labeled "Skeptic."

No external claims.

# Slide 4: Pattern 2 - Anxious

Main point: Anxiety usually needs clarity, boundaries, and follow-up.

Bullets:

- "Will this replace me?"
- "Will my work be monitored?"
- "Will I be blamed if it is wrong?"
- Response: anchor in commitments and review process

Speaker notes:

Practice naming what is known, what is not known, and where the staff member can get follow-up.

Image guidance:

Scenario card labeled "Anxious."

Evidence and review notes:

Use approved commitment language only.

# Slide 5: Pattern 3 - Overenthusiast

Main point: Enthusiasm can create risk when staff skip review or policy.

Bullets:

- "The tool can do all of this"
- "I already automated it"
- "The answer looked right"
- Response: slow down, review, route through intake

Speaker notes:

Overenthusiasm is as important as anxiety. Managers should coach enthusiastic staff toward governance, not shame them for experimenting.

Image guidance:

Scenario card labeled "Overenthusiast."

Evidence and review notes:

Align with AUP and intake process.

# Slide 6: Pattern 4 - Passive

Main point: Passive resistance often needs a small, specific next step and a follow-up date.

Bullets:

- "I do not have time"
- "This is not my job"
- "I will wait until it is required"
- Response: one low-risk trial and follow-up

Speaker notes:

The manager's job is to lower the activation energy. Pick one specific task, one approved tool, one review step, and one follow-up date.

Image guidance:

Scenario card labeled "Passive."

Evidence and review notes:

Confirm local expectations before use.

# Slide 7: Healthy vs. Unhealthy Adoption Signals

Main point: Managers should look for behavior patterns, not just positive or negative attitudes.

Bullets:

- Healthy: questions and safe practice
- Healthy: reporting bad outputs
- Unhealthy: hidden tool use
- Unhealthy: skipping review
- Unhealthy: refusing to discuss workflows

Speaker notes:

This helps managers respond proportionately. Skepticism can be healthy; hidden overuse can be risky.

Image guidance:

# Slide 8: The Coaching Loop

Main point: Good coaching follows a repeatable pattern.

Bullets:

- Listen
- Validate what is real
- Name the policy or commitment
- Pick the next safe action
- Follow up by date

Speaker notes:

This loop works across all four patterns. It keeps managers from improvising or debating.
