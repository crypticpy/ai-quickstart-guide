# Slide 1: AI And Your Team

Main point: Managers need consistent language for the AI questions staff will ask them directly.

Bullets:

- Staff will ask managers first
- Improvised answers can sound like policy
- This session gives you language and practice
- The goal is trust, not persuasion

Speaker notes:

Open by naming the real moment: a staff member asks "is AI going to take my job?" in a one-on-one, hallway, or team meeting. The manager's answer will carry more weight than a program email.

Neutral manager one-on-one conversation in an office or virtual meeting setting. Avoid anxious or fear-based imagery.

# Slide 3: Start With The Commitment

Main point: Managers should anchor staff conversations in the signed leadership commitment, not personal reassurance.

Bullets:

- Use the actual commitment letter
- Name the date and scope
- Do not paraphrase beyond what is approved
- Follow up when you do not know

Speaker notes:

Read the local commitment letter aloud during the session. Managers need to hear the same words staff will hear. If the letter says no AI-driven layoffs through a defined period, use that exact language and date. Do not add promises HR or leadership did not approve.

Image guidance:

Document graphic labeled "Leadership commitment letter" with date and signature fields. Do not invent a seal.

Evidence and review notes:

Replace this slide with the actual commitment language or attach the letter. Confirm HR and legal approval before presenting.

# Slide 4: Augmentation vs. Automation

Main point: Most Year 1 use cases should help staff perform work, not remove staff judgment or accountability.

Bullets:

- Augmentation: AI helps a person do work
- Automation: AI performs a task with less direct human work
- Accountability remains with the agency
- Higher-risk automation needs deeper review

Speaker notes:

Use examples from the managers' teams. A drafting assistant is augmentation. A system that sends a benefit denial without review is not the same category and should trigger governance review.

Image guidance:

Two-column comparison: "AI helps staff" and "AI acts with less human involvement." Keep it plain and non-alarming.

Evidence and review notes:

No external claims.

# Slide 6: Role Evolution Map

Main point: Staff need to see what changes for their role, not a generic statement that "AI will help."

Bullets:

- What stays human
- What may get faster
- What becomes possible
- What new skills are needed

Speaker notes:

Walk through one role evolution map. For role families not already covered, build a draft in the room. The best map is specific enough that a staff member recognizes their work.

Image guidance:

Four-quadrant role map with the labels from the bullets.

Evidence and review notes:

Use role maps reviewed by HR or program leadership where available.

# Slide 7: Scenario 1 - Is AI Going To Take My Job?

Main point: The manager should answer from the commitment letter and the role map.

Bullets:

- Refer to the commitment letter
- Name what stays human
- Name what may change
- Offer a follow-up role-map discussion

Speaker notes:

Practice until the answer sounds like the manager, not like a script. The answer should not minimize the concern. It should be clear, direct, and bounded by the actual commitment.

Image guidance:

Conversation prompt card. No image needed beyond a clean scenario layout.

Evidence and review notes:

Confirm exact commitment language and dates.

# Slide 8: Scenario 2 - I Tried It Once And It Was Wrong

Main point: Managers should validate the failure and coach toward safer, lower-stakes practice.

Bullets:

- Acknowledge that AI can be wrong
- Treat detection as a skill
- Ask for one more low-stakes try
- Invite the staff member to share what failed

Speaker notes:

The miss to avoid is defensiveness. Do not say "you used the wrong tool" as the first response. A hallucinated answer is a real experience and an important learning moment.

Image guidance:

Simple prompt card with "Validate, explain, retry safely."

Evidence and review notes:

No external claims.

# Slide 9: Scenario 3 - I Do Not Want To Use AI

Main point: Adoption is stronger when managers respect autonomy and ask for small, low-risk practice.

Bullets:

- Do not argue
- Confirm expectations for approved tools
- Ask for one specific low-stakes trial
- Leave room for honest feedback

Speaker notes:

Some staff resistance is rational. The manager's job is not to win an argument. It is to create a safe path for practice and feedback while following agency expectations.

Image guidance:

Minimal scenario card. Use calm colors and no pressure imagery.

Evidence and review notes:

Confirm local HR and performance expectations before presenting.

# Slide 10: Scenario 4 - I Am Worried About Bias, Privacy, Or Surveillance

Main point: Some concerns are correct and should improve the program.

Bullets:

- Validate the concern as real
- Name the review path
- Offer an escalation option
- Invite participation where appropriate

Speaker notes:

The miss to avoid is dismissing concerns as fear. Staff may see risks leadership missed. Managers should know where to route concerns: Review Committee, privacy officer, equity officer, HR, legal, ombuds, or another local role.
