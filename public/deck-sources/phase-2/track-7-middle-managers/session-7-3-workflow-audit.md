# Slide 1: Workflow Audit

Main point: Managers are close enough to the work to identify AI-fit workflows responsibly.

Bullets:

- Start from real work
- Name pain points
- Identify data and risk
- Prioritize intake candidates

Speaker notes:

The session output is not a polished project plan. It is a prioritized list of workflows ready for intake or a clear finding that nothing is suitable yet.

# Slide 2: What We Are Looking For

Main point: Good AI-fit workflows are repetitive, document-heavy, searchable, or classification-heavy.

Bullets:

- Drafting or rewriting
- Summarizing or extracting
- Searching approved sources
- Routing or classifying
- Checking completeness

Speaker notes:

These are patterns, not guarantees. The workflow still needs data, risk, and review assessment.

# Slide 3: What We Are Not Looking For

Main point: Some workflows are poor first candidates even if AI sounds relevant.

Bullets:

- High-stakes decisions
- Poorly understood processes
- No human review point
- Sensitive data with unclear controls
- No owner or user group

Speaker notes:

This keeps the audit grounded. A "no for now" is useful output.

# Slide 4: The Workflow Audit Questions

Main point: The audit should describe the current process before naming AI.

Bullets:

- What work happens today?
- Who does it?
- How often?
- What slows it down?
- What data is used and who is affected?

Speaker notes:

Managers should avoid jumping to a tool. The workflow is the unit of analysis.

# Slide 5: Data And Public Impact

Main point: Data class and impact determine whether a workflow can move quickly.

Bullets:

- Public or internal data moves faster
- Confidential or regulated data needs deeper review
- Public-facing outputs need extra care
- Rights-impacting workflows are high risk

Speaker notes:

Managers do not need perfect classification. They need to flag uncertainty and avoid minimizing risk.

Image guidance:

Data and impact grid.

Evidence and review notes:

Align with local data classification and risk tiers.

# Slide 6: Human Review Point

Main point: Every candidate needs a clear human checkpoint.

Bullets:

- Who checks the AI output?
- What do they compare it against?
- What can they override?
- What gets logged or recorded?

Speaker notes:

If no one can explain the review point, the workflow is not ready for intake as an AI candidate.

# Slide 7: Frequency x Pain x Suitability

Main point: Managers prioritize workflows by how often they happen, how painful they are, and how suitable they are for AI support.

Bullets:

- Frequency: how often it happens
- Pain: time, backlog, rework, frustration
- Suitability: drafting, summarizing, search, extraction, routing
- Intake review still checks risk, readiness, and ownership

Speaker notes:

Use a simple high/medium/low score. False precision is not needed. Risk, readiness, and sponsor strength still matter, but they belong in intake review after managers identify promising workflows.

# Slide 8: Activity - Build Your Top Three

Main point: Each manager should leave with three prioritized intake submissions or a documented reason not to submit.

Bullets:

- List candidate workflows
- Score frequency, pain, and suitability
- Pick top three
- Draft intake summaries and mark unknowns
- Submit live if the intake path is open

Speaker notes:

If a manager finds no good candidates, capture that. Do not manufacture weak use cases. When the intake path is available, the session output is three live submissions per manager.

# Slide 10: Manager Follow-Through

Main point: Managers own the bridge between team pain points and reviewed AI opportunities.

Bullets:

- Confirm submissions or saved drafts
- Talk with staff about the workflows
- Watch for shadow AI
- Follow up on acknowledgment within 14 days
- Bring blockers to [program lead/channel]

Speaker notes:

Close with deadlines and support. The 14-day follow-up is for acknowledgment, clarification, and manager-team discussion, not for delaying the session output.
