# Slide 1: Governance As Enabler

Main point: AI governance is what makes reviewed adoption possible.

Bullets:

- Governance turns uncertainty into process
- Review makes risk visible
- Conditions can enable "yes"
- Today's decision: charter the Review Committee

Speaker notes:

Open by reframing governance. It is not a brake by default. It is the path that lets the agency approve useful AI with defensible conditions.

# Slide 2: What Happens Without Governance

Main point: Without a clear review path, useful ideas stall and risky ideas go around the process.

Bullets:

- Shadow AI grows
- Vendor pilots bypass review
- Legal and security see issues too late
- Leaders cannot defend decisions

Speaker notes:

Keep the tone pragmatic. This is not about blame. It is about avoiding a situation where every AI question becomes an emergency exception.

# Slide 3: What Governance Should Enable

Main point: Good governance helps the agency say "yes, with conditions" more often.

Bullets:

- Clear use rules
- Risk-based review
- Vendor questions before procurement
- Human review expectations
- Records of decisions

Speaker notes:

Executives should see governance as throughput. A good review process is predictable enough that departments know what to prepare.

# Slide 4: The Risk Tier Lens

Main point: Review depth should match public impact and data sensitivity.

Bullets:

- Tier 1: low-risk support
- Tier 2: meaningful operational impact
- Tier 3: rights, benefits, safety, employment, liberty, or money
- Higher tier means deeper review, not automatic rejection

Speaker notes:

Use the local tier model. If no model exists yet, frame this as the model the committee will finalize.

Image guidance:

Three-tier ladder with examples.

Evidence and review notes:

Align with local risk classification.

# Slide 5: What The Review Committee Does

Main point: The committee creates a practical forum for AI decisions that cross policy, legal, security, data, and operations.

Bullets:

- Reviews intake candidates
- Sets conditions for pilots
- Escalates high-risk issues
- Maintains decision records
- Updates standards as learning accumulates

Speaker notes:

Make clear the committee is not a standing veto machine. It is a cross-functional decision body with a defined scope.

# Slide 8: Changing Legal And Reporting Context

Main point: Governance helps the agency respond when AI laws, reporting duties, procurement rules, and public expectations change.

Bullets:

- Track current legal and policy obligations
- Map obligations to committee review
- Update procurement and reporting artifacts
- Verify with counsel before relying on claims

Speaker notes:

Keep this concise and non-legalistic. The point is that executives need a review body that can absorb new requirements without reinventing the program each time.

Image guidance:

Compliance tracker feeding into Review Committee decisions.

Evidence and review notes:

Do not cite current-law obligations unless verified against official sources and local counsel.

# Slide 9: Common Executive Concerns

Main point: The governance model should answer predictable concerns before they block adoption.

Bullets:

- Who owns final risk?

Speaker notes:

Use this slide to surface objections. The answer is usually clear scope, escalation, and written decision records.
