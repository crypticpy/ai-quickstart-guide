- Substance portion: 30 minutes, followed by 20 minutes structured discussion and 10 minutes decision close
- Desired deck length: 12 slides
- Tone: concise, grounded, decision-oriented, low-jargon
- Format: 16:9 presentation
- Use speaker notes: yes
- Do not invent laws, statistics, citations, vendor claims, budget figures, or peer examples
- Keep the deck focused on the decision prompt: "What is our AI budget ceiling for Year 1?"
- Use visuals sparingly; this is an executive decision briefing, not a training class

# Slide 1: The AI Landscape - Hype vs. Reality

Main point: The agency needs a Year 1 budget posture grounded in what AI can actually deliver.

Bullets:

- Separate working use cases from vendor hype
- Understand the true cost shape
- Choose a Year 1 budget ceiling
- Leave with a written decision

Open by naming the decision prompt: by the end of this briefing, the room should either set a specific Year 1 budget ceiling or name the exact follow-up needed to set it. This is not a technology tour.

# Slide 3: What Is Real Today

Main point: The most dependable Year 1 use cases are bounded, reviewable, and close to existing staff workflows.

Bullets:

- Drafting and summarization
- Search and retrieval over known documents
- Triage and classification for lower-stakes routing
- Document extraction from structured intake
- Peer examples to add: [source/year]

Speaker notes:

Emphasize the pattern: narrow scope, known data, human review, and a clear operational owner. Bring one or two cited peer examples for each category if available; do not rely on vendor claims.

Image guidance:

Four simple tiles, one for each category. Use icons or neutral workflow illustrations.

Evidence and review notes:

Every peer example needs a source, year, and local relevance note. Do not present unsourced peer outcomes.

# Slide 4: What Not To Fund First

Main point: Some AI ideas are attractive in demos but poor Year 1 bets for most agencies.

Bullets:

- Autonomous agents making multi-step decisions
- Predictive scoring for high-stakes decisions
- Public chatbot replacing a call center
- Deepfake detection as a complete safeguard

Speaker notes:

This slide is about sequencing, not permanent rejection. Some of these ideas may become viable later with governance, procurement, evaluation, monitoring, and public-facing safeguards. They are usually not the first funded project.

Image guidance:

Simple "defer" list, not warning imagery. Keep the tone measured.

Evidence and review notes:

If local leaders want to discuss a specific vendor pitch, evaluate it separately using procurement guardrails and current evidence.

# Slide 5: The Real Cost Shape

Main point: AI program cost is mostly people, governance, evaluation, operations, and change management.

Bullets:

- Model usage is only one line item
- Staff time is often the largest cost
- Legal, procurement, security, and training are real costs
- Year 2 can grow as usage expands

Speaker notes:

Correct the "free with our cloud subscription" assumption directly. The model bill may be small at first. The program cost is the work around the model: review, testing, monitoring, training, support, and governance.

Image guidance:

Stacked cost graphic with people and operations as the largest blocks, model/API usage as a smaller block.

Evidence and review notes:

Use current local pay bands, contracts, and provider pricing before presenting numbers.

# Slide 6: Example Cost Model For One Moderate Use Case

Main point: A moderate internal AI use case can become a real operating commitment even when model usage is modest.

Bullets:

- Bounded policy-search assistant
- 50 staff users
- 12 months of operation
- Cost lines: model usage, retrieval, ingestion, evaluation, legal/procurement, support

Speaker notes:

Use this as a pattern, not a universal price. The source guide used a representative RAG-powered policy search example. Rebuild the number for your agency using current salary, cloud, model, support, and procurement assumptions.

Image guidance:

Simple table or waterfall: model usage, retrieval infrastructure, ingestion, staff evaluation, legal/procurement, support.

Evidence and review notes:

The source guide includes a representative range, but presentation numbers should be rebuilt against current provider pricing and local staffing costs before use.

# Slide 7: Quick Wins Worth Considering

Main point: A good first AI project should produce visible value while keeping scope and risk bounded.

Bullets:

- Drafting assistant: 4-6 month payback candidate
- Bounded RAG search: 4-6 month payback candidate
- Document extraction: 4-6 month payback candidate
- Meeting or board-minutes support: verify locally

Speaker notes:

Invite the room to map these categories to known operational pain. The point is not to choose the most impressive demo. The point is to choose the project with a real owner, real users, manageable data, and measurable value.

Image guidance:

Grid of quick-win options, each with "owner, users, data, review" as tiny footer labels.

Evidence and review notes:

No external claims unless local examples are added.

# Slide 8: Long Plays Need Foundations

Main point: Larger AI benefits compound after governance, infrastructure, development practice, and platform modules are in place.

Bullets:

- Internal AI platform: 12+ month compounding path
- Cross-department orchestration: needs foundations first
- Public-facing assistants: typically slower to govern
- High-stakes support: only after deeper controls

Speaker notes:

Frame this as investment sequencing. Executives do not need to reject the long plays; they need to stop funding them before the foundations exist.

Image guidance:

Simple staircase: governance, infrastructure, dev stack, platform, starter project, broader adoption.

Evidence and review notes:

No external claims.

# Slide 9: Shadow AI Is Already A Budget Issue

Main point: Unmanaged AI use creates risk and hidden demand even before the agency funds a program.

Bullets:

- Staff may already be using consumer tools
- Sensitive data rules may be unclear
- Useful demand is showing up outside intake
- Budget can turn shadow use into reviewed use

Speaker notes:

Ask the room where shadow AI is likely happening today. Keep the tone non-punitive. Most shadow AI starts because staff are trying to get work done, not because they are trying to evade governance.

Image guidance:

Simple funnel: informal use, intake, review, approved tool path.

Evidence and review notes:

Use local evidence if available. Do not accuse specific teams without verification.

# Slide 10: Discussion Prompts

Main point: The discussion should produce budget posture, not general opinions about AI.

Bullets:

- Where is AI already showing up?
- Which quick win maps to real pain?
- What budget range is defensible for Year 1?
- What conditions should trigger more funding?

Speaker notes:

Use these prompts in order. Capture answers visibly. If a debate starts drifting into tool features, bring it back to budget posture, risk appetite, and which first project the agency could support responsibly.

Image guidance:

Meeting-room discussion slide with four prompts in large type. No decorative imagery needed.

No external claims.
